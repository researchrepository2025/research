## 1. Healthcare

**Market Size:** $36.7B–$39.3B (2025) — [Fortune Business Insights](https://www.fortunebusinessinsights.com/industry-reports/artificial-intelligence-in-healthcare-market-100534) / [Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/artificial-intelligence-ai-healthcare-market) | Projected CAGR of ~39–44% through 2034. **Competitive Landscape:** Epic Systems dominates the EHR infrastructure layer with 42.3% of U.S. acute-care hospital market share (54.9% of acute-care beds) and $5.7B in 2024 revenue, having won nearly 70% of all hospital EHR decisions in 2024. Microsoft/Nuance's Dragon Copilot (DAX) holds 33% of the ambient scribe market across 600+ health systems, despite startups Abridge and Ambience collectively capturing ~70% of new ambient scribe contracts. Tempus AI is the fastest-growing pure-play, reaching $1.3B in FY2025 revenue (+83.4% YoY) with 35% of U.S. oncologists connected to its platform and 95% of top pharma companies using its services. GE Healthcare leads in FDA-cleared AI devices with 72 cleared products across a $19.6B total revenue base. Aidoc is the leading radiology AI pure-play, supporting 150+ health systems with 30 FDA-cleared authorizations and $60M in 2025 revenue.

## Cross-Segment Themes

### 1. Regulatory Moats as Competitive Barriers

Healthcare and Insurance present the highest regulatory barriers in the vertical AI landscape. In Healthcare, FDA clearance creates durable differentiation: GE Healthcare holds 72 FDA-cleared AI devices and Aidoc holds 30, with each clearance representing 12–18+ months of validation work. In Insurance, ZestyAI's AI-driven wildfire/storm models have been approved in 50+ regulatory filings, and Lemonade's AI Jim claims automation operates under state insurance commissioner oversight. In Government, security clearance levels (Palantir's IL6, Booz Allen's cleared workforce) function as structural entry barriers. In Legal, bar association guidance on AI use is creating a bifurcation between firms that invest in compliance infrastructure (Harvey, Thomson Reuters) and those that cannot.

### 2. Data Moats and Proprietary Training Sets

The most defensible vertical AI companies have exclusive access to proprietary data. Epic's CoMET model was pretrained on 118 million patients and 115 billion discrete medical events — a dataset no new entrant can replicate. Tempus AI has genomic data from approximately 35% of U.S. oncologists and 95% of top pharma companies. FICO's Scores segment benefits from decades of consumer credit data. Zillow's Zestimate is trained on transaction data from 80% of residential real estate transactions. CoStar's Matterport acquisition added 50.7 billion sq ft of digitized space, an asset accumulating since 2011. These data estates function as compounding moats unavailable via compute alone.

### 3. Incumbent vs. Startup Dynamics

The pattern across verticals is consistent: startups capture new buying occasions while incumbents retain renewal relationships. In Healthcare, 85% of generative AI spend flows to startups per Menlo Ventures, yet Epic retains 42.3% of the EHR infrastructure. In Legal, Harvey AI grew from $0 to $195M ARR in roughly three years while Thomson Reuters/LexisNexis retain distribution across millions of practitioners. In Education, Duolingo and Coursera captured the AI upside while Chegg, which failed to adapt, lost 99% of its peak market cap. In Manufacturing, o9 Solutions is growing subscription revenue 37% YoY while SAP and Oracle remain the named leaders in supply chain AI per MarketsandMarkets but cannot isolate AI revenue from ERP base. The consistent pattern: incumbents hold more total revenue; startups hold more AI-specific growth and valuation multiples.

### 4. Network Effects and Marketplace Dynamics

Several verticals exhibit network effects that compound winner-take-most dynamics. ServiceTitan's $55.7B in gross transaction volume creates a data loop that improves Titan Intelligence's AI recommendations. Angi's homeowner-contractor marketplace, operating in the $600B home services market, depends on liquidity at both sides. CoStar's Homes.com achieved $100M annualized revenue from 31,000 agent subscribers while generating 2.1 billion listing views, a marketplace flywheel reinforced by the Matterport 3D digital twin layer. In Finance, Socure's identity verification network improves with each of the 2.7B annual identity requests it processes.

### 5. Platform Consolidation and Vertical Suite Strategy

The most aggressive consolidation is occurring in Home Services (ServiceTitan acquiring FieldRoutes), Real Estate (CoStar acquiring Matterport), Finance (Visa acquiring Featurespace), and Insurance (CCC's platform connecting 35,000+ businesses). In each case, the leading platform is expanding horizontally to capture adjacent workflows rather than building point solutions. This mirrors the horizontal AI trend of CRM suites (Salesforce Agentforce) bundling across departmental workflows.

### 6. Speed of New Contract Formation as Leading Indicator

Harvey AI growing from $50M to $195M ARR in 12 months, EliseAI doubling revenue for four consecutive years, Tempus AI growing 83% YoY, Upstart's Q2 2025 loans up 159% YoY, and CoStar's Homes.com reaching $100M annualized ARR from a standing start collectively indicate that vertical AI adoption in 2025 is not incremental — in the segments where workflow fit exists, contract formation is accelerating geometrically. The vertical segments with the clearest workflow fit (legal document review, medical imaging triage, insurance claims processing, supply chain planning) are producing the steepest ARR acceleration curves.

### 7. Public vs. Private Revenue Visibility

A notable asymmetry exists across verticals in revenue transparency. In Healthcare, Tempus AI went public in June 2024 (ticker: TEM), providing audited quarterly data. In Home Services, ServiceTitan IPO'd in December 2024 (TTAN). In Insurance, CCC and Lemonade are public (CCCS, LMND). But the highest-growth pure-plays — Harvey AI, EliseAI, Midjourney, Runway, Shift Technology, o9 Solutions — are private with self-reported or third-party estimated figures. Revenue data for these companies should be treated with appropriate uncertainty.
