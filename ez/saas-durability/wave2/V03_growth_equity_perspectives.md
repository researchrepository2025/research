# V03: Growth Equity and Late-Stage Investor Perspectives on Enterprise SaaS Durability

**Research Wave:** Wave 2 — Investor Positioning
**Scope:** Growth equity firms, late-stage venture, and PE buyout firms; excludes early-stage VC and public market analysis
**Date Compiled:** March 2026
**Sources:** 2025–2026 unless marked [PRE-2025]

---

## Executive Summary

Growth equity and late-stage investors entered 2026 with a bifurcated posture on enterprise SaaS. Private equity—led by Thoma Bravo and Vista Equity—is doubling down on software buyouts with record capital raises, betting that domain-entrenched SaaS companies will absorb AI rather than be destroyed by it. Growth equity firms such as Insight Partners raised fresh billion-dollar funds while explicitly redefining their thesis around "agentic SaaS." Tiger Global, scarred by a $12.7B fund that fell 20% before recovering, shrank its next vehicle to $2.2B and warned that "valuations are elevated, and, in our view, at times unsupported by company fundamentals." General Atlantic lost its long-time tech leader Anton Levy, who departed to launch an AI-first firm targeting 300% annual growth companies.

The market context sharpened in February 2026 when a software selloff erased over $1 trillion in market capitalization in a single week, with the IGV ETF dropping nearly 20% year-to-date. Against this backdrop, late-stage deal flow is concentrating in AI-native and vertically-differentiated SaaS, while horizontal point-solution vendors face both a shrinking investor pool and structural pressure on seat-based pricing models.

The central PE thesis: SaaS with 30 years of domain expertise in regulated verticals is not a candidate for displacement—it is a candidate for acquisition at distressed multiples.

## Key Takeaways

[FACT]
Thoma Bravo's position is explicit: domain-specific SaaS with 30+ years of accumulated workflow expertise is "really, really cheap right now" and represents a generational buying opportunity. The firm committed $34.4 billion in fresh capital and closed approximately $42 billion in software acquisitions in 2025 alone.

[FACT]
Vista Equity's position is explicit: "AI will enable enterprise software to eat services." More than half of Vista portfolio companies are already monetizing AI; 30 are deploying agents in-market. The firm's Agentic AI Factory is a structural initiative to prevent AI from competing with portfolio companies and instead to make those companies AI-delivery vehicles.

[FACT]
Insight Partners' position is explicit: SaaS as a category persists, but the archetype transitions from static workflow software to "agentic SaaS." The firm raised $12.5 billion and made 85 investments in 2025. Its Managing Directors publicly distinguish between SaaS that is a "filing cabinet" (vulnerable) and SaaS that is a "system of work" (durable).

[FACT]
Tiger Global's position: disciplined retreat. From 300+ deals annually to 9 new investments in 2025. The PIP 17 fund letter explicitly warns that "valuations are elevated, and, in our view, at times unsupported by company fundamentals." The firm's prior SaaS-heavy portfolio suffered mass write-downs, portfolio company closures, and bankruptcy (Byju's).

[FACT]
The late-stage SaaS exit market is structurally broken for horizontal, AI-light companies: PE is not bidding unless the target is in a hot vertical, shows Rule of 40+, has AI deeply integrated, or has a clear path to 40%+ EBITDA margins. Software companies are "selling closer to 15x EBITDA" rather than "25x EBITDA anymore" (Jason Lemkin, SaaStr, 2025).
