## Overall Wave 2 Assessment

**Overall Score: PASS -- High Quality**

Wave 2 is a strong research deliverable. The seven files collectively provide comprehensive coverage of the VC/investor landscape on SaaS durability from 2024 through early March 2026. Attribution quality is consistently high across all files -- named investors, titles, affiliations, dates, and URLs are present for virtually every factual claim. The intentional bear/bull separation (V04/V05) works well structurally. Source quality is predominantly Tier 1-2 (firm publications, Fortune, Bloomberg, TechCrunch, Crunchbase, PitchBook).

### Notable Strengths

1. **Attribution rigor.** This wave handles the single hardest requirement -- accurately attributing named investor positions with full context -- consistently well across all seven files.

2. **Internal contradictions surfaced.** V01 explicitly identifies Sarah Wang's contradictory positions. V02 identifies the Lin vs. Grady/Huang split at Sequoia. V04 includes bears' own self-corrections (Palihapitiya's "year of letdowns"). This level of intellectual honesty strengthens credibility.

3. **Investment behavior matched to rhetoric.** Multiple files (V01, V02, V03, V06) ground opinion in capital allocation data, preventing the research from becoming a collection of unanchored quotes.

4. **V07's conditional framework.** The six-condition "It Depends On" matrix is the most synthesis-ready output in the wave and will be essential for the final report.

5. **Timeliness.** Sources extend through March 5, 2026, capturing the February 2026 SaaSpocalypse selloff and its immediate analytical aftermath.

### Gaps for Downstream Synthesis

1. **Non-US investor perspectives.** The wave is heavily US-centric. European VCs (beyond Lightspeed India, DTCP, and Accel Europe mentions) and Asian investor perspectives are essentially absent. SoftBank Vision Fund gets one mention (deal volume decline) but no thesis content.

2. **LP perspective.** Limited Partners -- the investors who fund VCs -- are entirely absent. LP sentiment on SaaS allocations would add a structural layer to the capital flow analysis.

3. **Public market investor depth.** V07 touches on equity research (BofA, Janus Henderson) but systematic coverage of public market software analysts (Morgan Stanley's Keith Weiss is mentioned but barely quoted; Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan absent) would strengthen the selloff analysis.

4. **Quantified returns data.** While V03 covers Tiger Global losses and V06 covers valuation multiples, the wave lacks systematic data on actual VC fund returns from SaaS portfolios vs. AI-native portfolios. This would be the strongest possible evidence for whether the capital reallocation is rational.

5. **Time-series consistency.** Some statistics across files use slightly different methodologies or date ranges for the same metric (e.g., AI share of VC funding appears as 34%, 37%, 45%, 50%, 53%, and 64% depending on geography, time period, and source). Downstream synthesis should reconcile these into a consistent narrative.

6. **Missing voice: Founders Fund / Peter Thiel.** Founders Fund led Cognition's $400M raise and is mentioned in V06 but has no thesis content. Given Thiel's historical influence on tech investing narratives, this is a notable gap.
