## 1. Wave Theme

The overarching story the VC and investor community tells in Wave 2 is one of violent internal disagreement masquerading as consensus. Nearly every major firm — a16z, Sequoia, Benchmark, Accel, Lightspeed — publicly states that software is not dying while simultaneously redirecting capital at a historic rate toward AI-native alternatives to the SaaS companies they once championed. The community has converged on a single structural claim: the per-seat SaaS pricing model is broken and outcome-based or usage-based pricing must replace it. Beyond that claim, camps fracture sharply. A disruption camp (led by voices at a16z, Benchmark, Bessemer, Lightspeed, Chamath Palihapitiya, Dean Shahar at DTCP) argues agentic coding tools have collapsed the cost of building custom software replacements to a fraction of legacy SaaS pricing — and that AI-native alternatives growing 5–10x faster than SaaS benchmarks will capture the category. A durability camp (Alfred Lin at Sequoia, Steven Sinofsky and Anish Acharya at a16z, Thoma Bravo's Orlando Bravo, Insight Partners' Ryan Hinkle and Praveen Akkiraju) argues that domain expertise, proprietary data, workflow lock-in in regulated industries, and the total irrationality of rebuilding 30-year-old ERP systems with vibe coding create moats far more durable than the market selloff implies. A nuanced middle camp (Bain, Forrester, Gartner, Jamin Ball, Tomasz Tunguz, Bank of America) concludes that both camps are correct about different software categories, and that the analytical error driving the February 2026 SaaSpocalypse — which wiped approximately $1 trillion in software market capitalization — was treating SaaS as a monolith rather than a heterogeneous portfolio of workflows with radically different AI displacement profiles. The capital flows, not the words, provide the most honest signal: AI captured approximately 50% of all global venture funding in 2025 versus 34% in 2024, while agentic coding tools alone attracted over $6 billion in a five-month window, and the median revenue multiple for AI-native companies (25–38x) now stands 4–5x above the median for traditional SaaS (5–7x).

## 2. Key Evidence for Each Scenario

### Bull Case (SaaS remains dominant, 60%+ market share in 5 years)

- [65% of Global 2000 CIOs surveyed by a16z prefer incumbent solutions when available, citing trust, integration, and procurement simplicity](https://a16z.com/leaders-gainers-and-unexpected-winners-in-the-enterprise-ai-arms-race/) — the most direct enterprise buyer preference data in this wave.

- [Gartner projects worldwide software spending will grow 14.7% in 2026 to more than $1.4 trillion, making software the fastest-growing segment of the $6.15 trillion IT market](https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/) — absolute spending expansion undermines market-share-collapse narratives.

- [Forrester projects global SaaS spending to rise from $318 billion in 2025 to $576 billion by 2029, with vertical software specifically growing from $133.5 billion to $194 billion over the same period](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/) — Forrester explicitly states "the reported death of SaaS is overstated" for domain-specific vendors.

- [Anish Acharya (a16z GP) stated the "vibe code everything" thesis is "flat wrong," noting software is only 8–12% of company expenses, so rebuilding payroll or ERP systems with AI-generated code saves only ~10% of total costs](https://benzatine.com/news-room/ai-coding-revolution-a16z-partner-questions-over-reliance-on-automation) — a direct economic rebuttal to the agentic coding displacement thesis.

- [SaaS companies on Carta raised $28.2 billion across the first three quarters of 2025, a 25% increase over the same period in 2024; in Q3 2025 alone, approximately 33% of all venture funding went to SaaS startups](https://carta.com/data/saas-industry-spotlight-Q3-2025/) — continued robust capital flow into the category despite the narrative.

- [Thoma Bravo committed $34.4 billion across three equity funds in June 2025, closing approximately $42 billion in SaaS acquisitions in 2025 alone, betting that companies with "30 years of domain expertise built into their product" are "really, really cheap right now"](https://capwolf.com/orlando-bravo-why-software-stocks-are-oversold-now/) — the largest software PE firm in the world is a structural buyer, not a seller.

- [Average gross retention for SaaS companies holding at approximately 90% or better through 2025–2026, with Bain noting the metric was "holding steady" even as net revenue retention stalled](https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/) — churn is not accelerating despite the selloff narrative.

- [Avenir survey data: 63% of enterprise buyers expect their existing software vendors to benefit from generative AI; only 8% expect them to lose](https://nextbigteng.substack.com/p/the-saasacre-of-2026) — enterprise buyer sentiment is significantly more constructive than public market pricing implies.

- [Only 6% of surveyed firms had AI agents in production as of October 2025 per BofA channel research](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645) — actual deployment pace is far behind the disruption narrative timeline.

### Base Case (SaaS stays majority but declines, 45–60% market share)

- [Bain identifies a "seat compression" dynamic as the most likely near-term mode of SaaS disruption: "Rather than 500 seats, customers might purchase 450 and deploy agents for marginal usage" — a margin headwind rather than category destruction](https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/) — quantifies the mechanism for a gradual market-share shift without extinction.

- [Gartner predicts that agentic AI could drive approximately 30% of enterprise application software revenue by 2035, surpassing $450 billion — up from 2% in 2025 — conditional on successful implementation scaling](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-08-26-gartner-predicts-40-percent-of-enterprise-apps-will-feature-task-specific-ai-agents-by-2026-up-from-less-than-5-percent-in-2025) — this implies traditional SaaS retains the remainder, a gradual share shift.

- [Deloitte predicts "parts of entire enterprise applications could eventually be replaced by agents, but it won't be in 2026 — it will likely take at least five years or more"](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html) — the disruption timeline is long enough for incumbents to adapt.

- [Gartner: 40% of enterprise SaaS will include outcome-based pricing elements by 2026, up from 15% a few years prior — indicating the pricing model shifts but the vendor relationships persist](https://blockchain.news/ainews/gartner-predicts-40-of-enterprise-software-will-feature-ai-agents-by-2026-disrupting-traditional-saas-pricing-models) — pricing evolution rather than vendor replacement.

- [Insight Partners' "agentic SaaS" thesis: "SaaS as we know it, but under the covers, agentic workflows injecting dynamism and intelligence into software that used to be static"](https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/2026-investor-predictions/) — the $90 billion AUM firm's official position is continuity of vendor relationship, transformation of product.

- [IDC: SaaS "is being disrupted, not by decline but by evolution"; by 2028, pure seat-based pricing will be obsolete and 70% of software vendors will refactor pricing strategies](https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/is-saas-dead-rethinking-the-future-of-software-in-the-age-of-ai/) — evolution framing explicitly contradicts extinction framing.

- [Bain: AI could automate between 30% and 50% of activity across enterprise functions, particularly "high-volume, highly digitized, lower-stakes activities such as handling customer support tickets, writing code, and answering inbound inquiries"](https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/) — partial automation of SaaS workflows, not wholesale replacement.

- [Javier Pérez (Edelweiss Capital Research) assigns 30–40% probability to "AI Enhances SaaS" and 20–30% to "AI Outshines SaaS" — the two moderate scenarios together dominate the probability distribution](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas) — the base case is the most likely single outcome.

### Bear Case (SaaS significantly eroded, <45% market share)

- [The Citrini Research "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" report (February 2026) posits that agentic coding tools reached a capability threshold in late 2025 enabling developers to replicate mid-market SaaS functionality in weeks; the report drove a 13% single-session drop in the S&P Software Index, wiping approximately $285 billion in market cap](https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic) — the market-moving bear thesis, whatever its credibility limitations.

- [Bessemer: LLM-native companies have quickly reached 80% of the average contract value of traditional core vertical SaaS systems, growing approximately 400% year-over-year while maintaining ~65% gross margins](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-state-of-ai-2025) — AI-native companies are achieving near-parity on ACV at a fraction of the development cost.

- [AI-native companies are achieving 6–12x better revenue efficiency than traditional SaaS; AI-driven SaaS companies grow 5–10x faster than SaaS-era benchmarks in the Benchmark Capital portfolio](https://developmentcorporate.com/saas/ai-revolution-counter-intuitive-truths-accel-2025/) — the competitive growth differential is documented, not hypothetical.

- [Traditional B2B SaaS funding dropped 60% year-over-year in Q4 2025 while AI-native enterprise companies raised record amounts](https://www.techbuzz.ai/articles/the-saaspocalypse-ai-agents-are-eating-enterprise-software) — capital allocation has already shifted dramatically.

- [The average number of SaaS applications used by an organization decreased from 112 to 106 by April 2025, with 82% of organizations actively reducing their number of software vendors](https://xpert.digital/en/the-big-reckoning) — vendor consolidation is already occurring.

- [Satya Nadella (Microsoft CEO): "The notion that business applications exist — that's probably where they'll all collapse in the Agent Era because if you think about it they are essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic"](https://www.windowscentral.com/microsoft/hey-why-do-i-need-excel-microsoft-ceo-satya-nadella-foresees-a-disruptive-agentic-ai-era-that-could-aggressively-collapse-saas-apps) — the single highest-credibility bear-case statement in this wave, coming from the CEO of the largest enterprise software company.

- [Klarna's replacement of Salesforce and Workday with custom AI solutions is described by a16z's Marc Andrusko as "cord-cutting" that will be "replicated many times over"](https://a16z.com/big-ideas-in-tech-2025/) — a documented enterprise precedent, not merely a hypothetical.

- [Deloitte projects 35% of point-product SaaS tools will be replaced by AI agents or absorbed by 2030](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html) — a Tier 1 consulting firm's quantified displacement estimate for the bear outcome.

- [PitchBook Q1 2026 analyst note "SaaS Is Dead, Long Live SaS": as ARC-AGI-2 benchmarks push past 60% labor-replacement milestones at $1–$10 per task, "an annual charge of $1,200 per seat becomes an annual charge of $10,000 per automated workflow" — the economic logic flips](https://pitchbook.com/news/reports/q1-2026-pitchbook-analyst-note-saas-is-dead-long-live-sas) — the capability threshold triggering economic displacement is named specifically.

## 4. Key Data Points for the Position Paper

1. **The TAM argument that reframes the entire debate:** ["The worldwide SaaS market is about $300 billion per year. The labor market in the U.S. alone is $13 trillion"](https://x.com/a16z/status/1974150330532389129) — Alex Rampell, a16z. If AI-native software addresses the labor TAM rather than the SaaS TAM, the competitive framing changes entirely. The SaaS "dominance" question becomes irrelevant if the category itself is the wrong unit of analysis.

2. **The valuation multiple chasm as a leading indicator:** [AI-native companies trade at a median revenue multiple of 25–38x; traditional SaaS trades at 5–7x — a 4–5x spread that has not narrowed](https://aventis-advisors.com/ai-valuation-multiples/). Capital markets are pricing a structural divergence, not a temporary rotation.

3. **The speed of AI-native growth versus SaaS-era benchmarks:** [Benchmark portfolio companies reaching $100M ARR "five to ten times as fast" as SaaS companies; LLM-native companies growing ~400% year-over-year at ~65% gross margins while reaching 80% of traditional SaaS ACV](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-state-of-ai-2025). Growth velocity, not market share, is the bear camp's primary evidentiary claim.

4. **Enterprise buyer preference is more conservative than VC narrative:** [65% of Global 2000 CIOs prefer incumbent solutions when available; enterprises budgeted $3.9M for AI apps but spent $6M (54% budget overrun) — indicating AI spending is additive, not substitutional, at the enterprise level](https://a16z.com/leaders-gainers-and-unexpected-winners-in-the-enterprise-ai-arms-race/) — a16z CIO survey of 100 enterprises, January 2026.

5. **The Citrini market event establishes the bear thesis has teeth:** [The S&P Software Index fell 13% in a single session on February 24, 2026 — "Black Tuesday" — wiping approximately $285 billion in software market cap on the publication of a single research report from a "little known firm"](https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic). Market vulnerability to the bear narrative is itself a data point about consensus uncertainty.

6. **Gartner's dual-sided finding defines the base case probability range:** [40% of enterprise apps will embed task-specific AI agents by 2026 (integration, not replacement) AND 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by 2027 due to "escalating costs, unclear business value or inadequate risk controls"](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027). Deployment is faster than expected; productive deployment is slower than deployment.

7. **The actual enterprise vendor consolidation trend:** [The average number of SaaS applications per organization decreased from 112 to 106 by April 2025, with 82% of organizations actively reducing their number of software vendors](https://xpert.digital/en/the-big-reckoning). Consolidation is real but the pace — 5.4% reduction — is evolutionary, not catastrophic.

8. **Capital concentration in agentic coding is the strongest structural signal:** [Cursor alone: $2.3 billion Series D at $29.3 billion valuation (November 2025); Lovable: $330 million at $6.6 billion; Cognition AI: $400 million at $10.2 billion; Poolside: $2 billion at $12 billion — all within a five-month window (July–November 2025)](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/13/cursor-ai-startup-funding-round-valuation.html). The tools enabling custom software replacement are among the most capitalized startups in history.

9. **The "domain knowledge, not code" rebuttal is backed by the largest PE capital raise ever:** [Thoma Bravo completed a $34.4 billion raise across three equity funds, the largest in the firm's history, while explicitly arguing "software is not at all about the code or about the technology — software is about your domain knowledge"](https://www.thomabravo.com/press-releases/thoma-bravo-completes-34.4-billion-fundraise). The firm closed approximately $42 billion in acquisitions in 2025 and holds portfolio companies generating $30 billion in annual revenue.

10. **The timeline conditional is critical for scenario framing:** [Deloitte: "Parts of entire enterprise applications could eventually be replaced by agents, but it won't be in 2026 — it will likely take at least five years or more"](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html); [Gartner: agentic AI could reach 30% of enterprise software revenue by 2035, up from 2% in 2025](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-08-26-gartner-predicts-40-percent-of-enterprise-apps-will-feature-task-specific-ai-agents-by-2026-up-from-less-than-5-percent-in-2025). The bear case requires 10 years; the 2–5 year question the position paper addresses may favor the base case.

## 5. Critical Caveats for Synthesis

### Structural Conflicts in VC Positions

**Words versus capital allocation.** The most important internal contradiction in this wave: VCs publicly state SaaS is not dying while simultaneously allocating 50% of all global venture capital to AI (up from 34% one year prior), assigning AI-native companies 4–5x the revenue multiple of SaaS companies, and explicitly naming horizontal SaaS without AI differentiation as the category losing VC share in 2026. The divergence between rhetoric and portfolio construction is the single most important analytical fact in this wave.

**Intra-firm disagreement is real, not performative.** At a16z, named partners are on record with contradictory theses: Rampell/George/Andrusko argue SaaS per-seat is dying; Sinofsky/Acharya argue vibe-coding replacements are economically irrational; Wang/Kahl/Xu co-author both "systems of record will lose primacy" and "the reported death of third party apps is greatly exaggerated." At Sequoia, Alfred Lin (co-steward) publicly contradicts Pat Grady (co-steward) on whether foundation models structurally threaten SaaS. These are not marketing disagreements — they reflect genuine uncertainty at the firms allocating the most capital.

**The Citrini credibility problem.** The most market-moving bear document in this wave was authored by a "little known firm" (Bloomberg's characterization), with an author who admitted the market reaction was "definitely larger than we expected." Citrini's 2028 scenario model contains projections (S&P 500 down 38%, unemployment at 10.2%) that are speculative macro scenarios, not enterprise software analysis. Using Citrini data points requires caveat about the source.

### Bias Considerations

**VCs have incentives on both sides.** Firms with large legacy SaaS portfolios (Sequoia's ServiceNow stake, Bessemer's Twilio/Shopify positions) have financial incentive to argue SaaS durability. Firms with new AI-native investments (a16z's $1.7 billion AI Apps allocation, Bessemer's $1 billion+ in AI-native startups) have financial incentive to argue AI disruption. Both camps' public positions should be read as partially self-serving.

**PE has an explicit "buy the dip" motive.** Thoma Bravo's Orlando Bravo calling SaaS companies "really, really cheap right now" while simultaneously deploying $42 billion in SaaS acquisitions in 2025 is a buyer talking his book. His bearishness on AI hype and bullishness on domain-specific SaaS are structurally aligned with his acquisition strategy.

**The disruption narrative serves AI-native startup fundraising.** Many of the most aggressive "SaaS is dead" voices (Chamath Palihapitiya, Dean Shahar) are either building or investing in AI-native alternatives. Their public positions are not independently verified market forecasts — they are partially competitive messaging.

### Data Quality Issues

**The SaaS market size figures are inconsistent across sources.** Gartner reports $300 billion worldwide SaaS end-user spending in 2025; Statista projects $428.78 billion; Forrester says $318 billion. These reflect different definitional scopes (pure-play SaaS vs. cloud software broadly defined). Market share calculations will be sensitive to which baseline is chosen.

**"AI-native" versus "AI-enhanced SaaS" is not consistently defined.** A Salesforce with AI agents embedded may be classified as SaaS by some analysts and as AI-native by others. The category boundary problem is severe enough that market-share estimates are not directly comparable across sources.

**Growth rates may overstate structural change.** Eric Vishria (Benchmark) explicitly cautioned that much of the AI-native revenue growth is "experimental revenue" — MRR run-rated to ARR — rather than contracted multi-year commitments comparable to legacy SaaS ARR. The growth rate differential may be partially a measurement artifact.

**The February 2026 SaaSpocalypse is likely sentiment-driven, not fundamentals-driven.** BofA's Brad Sills noted that the negative stock reactions "appear disconnected to the fundamentals"; Jason Lemkin argued the 2026 crash was primarily the market "pricing in the deceleration that started in 2021," not an AI-specific disruption event. The selloff data should be used carefully — it shows sentiment, not realized disruption.

## 6. Wave Verdict

The VC and investor wave provides strong evidence for the **base case** (SaaS remains the majority procurement method but loses share gradually to 45–60% range) and meaningful — but not decisive — evidence for both the bull and bear extremes. The bull case is supported by the largest bodies of enterprise buyer survey data (65% CIO incumbent preference, Avenir's 63% buyer optimism, 90%+ gross retention), the absolute spending growth projections (Gartner: +14.7% software spend in 2026), and the capital commitment of the world's largest software PE acquirer (Thoma Bravo's $34.4 billion raise paired with an explicit "domain expertise wins" thesis). The bear case is supported by the most dramatic leading indicators: a 4–5x valuation multiple divergence between AI-native and traditional SaaS, the 60% YoY decline in traditional B2B SaaS funding in Q4 2025, Satya Nadella's explicit prediction of business application collapse in the Agent Era, and documented vendor consolidation already reducing enterprise software stack sizes. However, the bear case's primary evidence — the Citrini report, Chamath Palihapitiya's 8090, the February 2026 SaaSpocalypse — suffers from credibility limitations (unknown firm, self-funded promoter, sentiment-driven selloff) that the position paper must acknowledge. The single most analytically useful finding from this wave is not a market share estimate but a framework: SaaS durability is not a binary question. It is conditional on software category (system of record vs. point solution), workflow type (deterministic vs. probabilistic), customer segment (large enterprise vs. SMB), and the pace at which incumbents can shift pricing models. On the 2–5 year timeline the position paper addresses, the nuanced camp's finding that full structural disruption requires 5+ years (Deloitte) while actual AI agent production deployment is at 6% (BofA) and 40%+ of agentic AI projects will be canceled by 2027 (Gartner) creates the most defensible base case: SaaS will remain the dominant enterprise procurement method in 2–5 years for mission-critical, vertically-integrated, and compliance-heavy applications, while losing significant ground in horizontal, UI-first, and point-solution categories — a market-share range of approximately 50–60%, not the sub-45% bear case and not the 60%+ bull case floor.
