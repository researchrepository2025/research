## Executive Summary

### The Hypothesis

The research question proposes that SaaS, despite the emergence of agentic coding tools capable of generating custom enterprise software, is unlikely to die within 2–5 years and will remain the dominant enterprise procurement modality for AI-driven applications — the primary method by which enterprises acquire business software capability rather than building it.

### The Verdict: Partially Confirmed at 87% Probability by 2027, 77% by 2030

The hypothesis is confirmed in its core claim but requires material qualification in three dimensions. SaaS will remain the majority enterprise procurement method for AI-driven applications at approximately **85% probability by 2027** and **75% probability by 2030** (revised from 87% and 77% respectively following Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026, which partially eroded one of five bear-case barrier conditions), defined as SaaS retaining greater than 50% of enterprise software application procurement spend. However, the "majority" projected by this research is a declining one: the base case — carrying 55% probability weight — projects SaaS share falling to 43–58% of enterprise software procurement by 2030, down from an estimated 65–70% today. The scenario framework assigns 25% probability to the bull case (SaaS dominant at 58–68% by 2030), 55% to the base case (SaaS retains majority but declines to 43–58% by 2030), and 20% to the bear case (SaaS falls below 43% by 2030, potentially losing majority status). Combined, the bull and base cases yield a 77–80% probability that SaaS holds majority status through 2030.

### Three Scenarios and Market Share Ranges

- **Bull (SaaS Adapts and Dominates):** 60–70% SaaS share by 2027; 58–68% by 2030. Probability: 25%.
- **Base (Hybrid Dominance with Meaningful Erosion):** 47–58% SaaS share by 2027; 43–55% by 2030. Probability: 55%.
- **Bear (Structural SaaS Displacement):** 30–45% SaaS share by 2027; 25–40% by 2030. Probability: 20%.

### Strongest Evidence for SaaS Durability

The three most important pieces of evidence supporting the hypothesis are: (1) **Enterprise execution reality lags the disruption narrative by 3–5 years** — only 6% of enterprises had AI agents in production as of October 2025 per Bank of America research ([https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645)), BCG finds only 5% of companies achieve AI value at scale ([https://media-publications.bcg.com/The-Widening-AI-Value-Gap-Sept-2025.pdf](https://media-publications.bcg.com/The-Widening-AI-Value-Gap-Sept-2025.pdf)), and Gartner predicts over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027 ([https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027)). (2) **Regulated, data-gravity enterprise SaaS remains structurally protected** — ERP custom-build failure rates of 55–75%, compliance certification cycles of 2–5 years for deployed production systems, and Epic's 16.3 billion patient encounters in its Cosmos dataset represent moats that agentic coding tools cannot replicate within the 5-year window. Note: Windsurf (formerly Codeium) achieved FedRAMP authorization in early 2026, making it the first agentic coding tool certified for federal government use. This removes the compliance-tool-access barrier for federal and regulated enterprise deployments but does not transfer certification to software built with that tool, which still requires a separate Authority to Operate process. (3) **Enterprise buy preference strengthened, not weakened, in 2025** — Menlo Ventures documents 76% of enterprise AI use cases being purchased rather than built in 2025, up from 53% in 2024 ([https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/)), and 65% of Global 2000 CIOs prefer incumbent vendors when available ([https://a16z.com/leaders-gainers-and-unexpected-winners-in-the-enterprise-ai-arms-race/](https://a16z.com/leaders-gainers-and-unexpected-winners-in-the-enterprise-ai-arms-race/)).

### Strongest Evidence Against the Hypothesis

The three most important counter-evidence points are: (1) **METR's task-horizon doubling trajectory** — autonomous AI task-completion time is doubling every 88.6–196.5 days ([https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/)), which extrapolates to agents capable of enterprise workday-length autonomous software projects by late 2026 to 2029, potentially crossing the economic feasibility threshold for custom builds well within the 5-year window. (2) **Documented displacement is already occurring in targeted categories** — 35% of enterprises have replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom build per Retool's 2026 report ([https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026)), standalone analytics declined 18 percentage points in IT budgets per BCG ([https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/it-spending-pulse-optimism-investment-and-priorities](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/it-spending-pulse-optimism-investment-and-priorities)), and Bain explicitly names HubSpot's list-building and Monday.com's task boards as already economically feasible substitution targets ([https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/)). (3) **SaaS pricing-as-extraction is approaching a political limit** — SaaS inflation runs at 12.2% annually versus 2.7% general CPI ([https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate)), and Salesforce extracted 72% of its 2025 ARR growth from price increases rather than new customers ([https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/](https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/)), a dynamic already driving CFO-level build mandates in targeted categories.

### What the Hypothesis Gets Right and What It Misses

The hypothesis correctly identifies the near-term reality: enterprise inertia, compliance moats, organizational governance immaturity, and the genuine difficulty of maintaining custom-built software at enterprise scale all protect SaaS's majority position through at least 2027. It correctly anticipates that most enterprises will continue buying, not building, their core software capability for the foreseeable future.

What the hypothesis misses, or understates, is the distinction between the *aggregate* market and the *category-level* story. Within the aggregate majority, specific SaaS categories — horizontal point solutions, workflow automation tools, standalone analytics, and internal admin tooling — are already experiencing displacement that Gartner and Deloitte project will reach 35% of the point-product market by 2030. The hypothesis also understates the speed of the pricing model disruption: the per-seat model is dying faster than the SaaS category itself, and conflating these produces overconfidence in SaaS durability. Finally, the hypothesis does not adequately account for the 2028–2030 window, when agentic capability maturation and enterprise governance improvements will converge in ways that could shift scenario probabilities materially — making the 5-year verdict genuinely more uncertain than the 2-year verdict.

## Part I: The Landscape — What We Know with High Confidence

### The Market Is Growing in Absolute Terms, But Its Composition Is Shifting

The foundational economic picture of enterprise SaaS is one of nominal growth masking structural strain. Forrester projects global SaaS spending growing from $318 billion in 2025 to $576 billion by 2029 ([https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/)). Gartner forecasts software spending growing 14.7% in 2026 to $1.4 trillion ([https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/](https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/)). Zylo finds the average enterprise SaaS spend growing to $55.7 million in 2026, up 8% year-over-year ([https://zylo.com/reports/2026-saas-management-index/](https://zylo.com/reports/2026-saas-management-index/)). These figures appear to tell a story of robust health. They require qualification.

First, Vertice's SaaS Inflation Index — based on over $1 billion in managed contracts — documents SaaS price increases running at 12.2% annually, 4.5 times the general CPI rate of 2.7% ([https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate)). Gartner's own John-David Lovelock, Distinguished VP Analyst at Gartner, attributed much of the spending growth to AI feature bundling rather than procurement expansion: "GenAI features are now ubiquitous across software enterprises already own and these features cost more. The cost of software is going up" ([https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-22-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-9-point-8-percent-in-2026-exceeding-6-trillion-dollars-for-the-first-time](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-22-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-9-point-8-percent-in-2026-exceeding-6-trillion-dollars-for-the-first-time)). If the 12.2% inflation accounts for approximately 12 percentage points of the reported 14.7% software spending growth, the real-terms volume expansion is approximately 2–3 percent — a very different story from the headline. Second, Zylo's dataset of 40 million licenses and $40 billion in managed spend finds that 52.7% of purchased SaaS licenses go unused ([https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/](https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/)), implying a substantial portion of the spending growth represents waste expansion rather than value creation. The correct analytical frame is to separate the absolute dollar figures from the market share trajectory: SaaS spending is growing, but a growing fraction of that growth is inflation and waste rather than adoption expansion.

### The Per-Seat Pricing Model Is Structurally Dead — But This Is Not Category Death

All nine independent research waves in this study reached the same conclusion: seat-based SaaS pricing is not surviving as the dominant commercial model. Forrester documents seat-based pricing falling from 21% to 15% of SaaS market share in a single year ([https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/)). IDC projects 70% of vendors will refactor their pricing models by 2028 ([https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/is-saas-dead-rethinking-the-future-of-software-in-the-age-of-ai/](https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/is-saas-dead-rethinking-the-future-of-software-in-the-age-of-ai/)). BCG finds 40% of IT buyers cite seat reduction as their primary near-term cost lever ([https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/rethinking-b2b-software-pricing-in-the-era-of-ai](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/rethinking-b2b-software-pricing-in-the-era-of-ai)). The mechanism is straightforward: AI-enhanced workers accomplish more with fewer licenses, and AI agents can handle marginal workflows without generating a seat. Bain describes the dynamic as "seat compression — rather than 500 seats, customers might purchase 450 and deploy agents for marginal usage" ([https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/](https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/)).

This pricing model disruption is real, consequential for vendor revenue, and proceeding faster than most vendors planned. However, it is analytically distinct from SaaS category displacement. The largest SaaS incumbents — Salesforce at $2.9 billion combined AI and Data ARR growing 200% year-over-year with 29,000 Agentforce deals ([https://investor.salesforce.com/news/news-details/2026/Salesforce-Delivers-Record-Fourth-Quarter-Fiscal-2026-Results/default.aspx](https://investor.salesforce.com/news/news-details/2026/Salesforce-Delivers-Record-Fourth-Quarter-Fiscal-2026-Results/default.aspx)), ServiceNow at 98% renewal rates and $28.2 billion in remaining performance obligations ([https://futurumgroup.com/insights/servicenow-q4-fy-2025-earnings-highlight-ai-platform-momentum/](https://futurumgroup.com/insights/servicenow-q4-fy-2025-earnings-highlight-ai-platform-momentum/)) — are demonstrating that the SaaS vendor relationship can be preserved and deepened even as the per-seat model dies. The pricing transition is a business model disruption that the category's dominant players are absorbing. The category displacement question — whether enterprises stop buying and start building — is a separate and harder question, with a different and more contested evidence base.

The position paper's entire analytical architecture rests on keeping this distinction intact. Conflating pricing model death with category death is the central definitional error in the "SaaS is dead" discourse, and it produces conclusions that are systematically overstated.

### Enterprise Execution Reality Lags the Disruption Narrative by 3–5 Years

The dissonance between the AI disruption narrative and enterprise deployment reality is perhaps the most practically important finding in this research. Bank of America found only 6% of surveyed firms had AI agents in production as of October 2025 ([https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645)). A Morgan Stanley survey of 150 CIOs found only 4 raised their hands when asked about agentic AI deployment in enterprise settings ([https://fintool.com/app/research/analyst/sanjit-singh](https://fintool.com/app/research/analyst/sanjit-singh)). Deloitte's State of AI report, based on 3,235 organizations, found only 21% have mature governance models for autonomous agents ([https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html)). BCG's Widening AI Value Gap study across 1,250 firms found only 5% are achieving AI value at scale, with 60% generating no material value ([https://media-publications.bcg.com/The-Widening-AI-Value-Gap-Sept-2025.pdf](https://media-publications.bcg.com/The-Widening-AI-Value-Gap-Sept-2025.pdf)). S&P Global found 42% of AI initiatives were abandoned before reaching production, a figure that surged from 17% in 2024, suggesting that the rate of enterprise AI project failure is accelerating, not declining ([https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/ai-experiences-rapid-adoption-but-with-mixed-outcomes-highlights-from-vote-ai-machine-learning](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/ai-experiences-rapid-adoption-but-with-mixed-outcomes-highlights-from-vote-ai-machine-learning)).

These figures are independently corroborated across research methodologies and sponsor types. Their convergence constitutes the most robust near-term protection for SaaS market share: the organizational, governance, and quality infrastructure required to build and operate custom agentic software at enterprise scale simply does not exist at most enterprises in early 2026. Gartner, reflecting this reality, predicts over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027 ([https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027)). This does not mean the disruption trajectory is invalidated — high failure rates characterized early cloud adoption in 2010–2012 as well. It means the timeline for meaningful market share erosion is longer than the narrative implies.

### The February 2026 Equity Selloff Was a Sentiment Event, Not a Revenue Event

The approximately $1 trillion erosion in software market capitalization during the first week of February 2026 — triggered by a report from Citrini Research, described by Bloomberg as a "little known firm," whose author subsequently acknowledged the market reaction was "larger than we expected" — requires careful interpretation. J.P. Morgan characterized the underlying investor logic as "broken," identifying a logical contradiction in bear investor behavior ([https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/02/17/jp-morgan-research-says-broken-logic-is-driving-th/](https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/02/17/jp-morgan-research-says-broken-logic-is-driving-th/)). As of March 2026, no actual customer cancellation data, churn rates, or net revenue retention changes attributable directly to AI agent displacement have been found in primary financial sources ([https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas)). Enterprise SaaS gross retention holds near 90%. The software sector maintains a return on invested capital of 50.17% versus a weighted average cost of capital of 9.34% — fundamentals that do not reflect structural collapse.

The equity selloff is not meaningless as a signal: sentiment events can become self-fulfilling through reduced investment in SaaS tooling and accelerated build decisions. Goldman Sachs strategist Ben Snider drew an explicit analogy to the newspaper industry, a comparison whose specific claim — that the revenue erosion will lag the structural shift by years — is worth taking seriously even while acknowledging it has not been confirmed by operating metrics. The intellectually honest framing treats the equity repricing as a leading indicator that warrants serious attention, while insisting that the position paper's evidence standards require actual financial confirmation before elevating the bear case probability above 20%.

### The Build-vs-Buy Shift Is Real, But Geographically Bounded

The displacement that is actually occurring is precisely documented by the convergence of seven independent research waves: it is real, directional, and concentrated in a specific set of categories. Retool's 2026 Build vs. Buy Report, which despite its sampling bias (n=817, skewed toward builders) captures early-adopter enterprise behavior accurately, documents replacement already occurring in workflow automations (35%), internal admin tools (33%), business intelligence tools (29%), and CRMs (25%) ([https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026)). Retool's own CEO, David Hsu, provides the most important quantifying statement: "Nobody's ripping out Salesforce wholesale. What they're doing is replacing the specific piece of a tool that never quite fit" ([https://www.newsweek.com/nw-ai/enterprises-are-replacing-saas-faster-than-you-think-11521483](https://www.newsweek.com/nw-ai/enterprises-are-replacing-saas-faster-than-you-think-11521483)). This is the distinction between displacement at the *margins* — which is occurring — and displacement at the *core* — which is not. Gartner and Deloitte project 35% of point-product SaaS tools will be replaced by AI agents or absorbed within larger agent ecosystems by 2030 ([https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html)). That 35% of the point-product long tail is not 35% of the SaaS market — the largest SaaS categories by spend (ERP, HR/HCM, platform SaaS, regulated verticals) are structurally protected and collectively represent the majority of enterprise SaaS expenditure.

## Part II: The Three Scenarios

### BULL SCENARIO: SaaS Adapts and Dominates

**Market Share Range:** 60–70% of enterprise AI application procurement by 2027; 58–68% by 2030
**Probability: 23%** (revised from 25% following Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026, which partially invalidated two bull-case compliance moat arguments)

In this scenario, agentic AI tools accelerate the migration of enterprise software away from per-seat models but SaaS vendors — especially Tier 1 platforms and vertical specialists — successfully absorb the transition by embedding AI agents into their own offerings and capturing the AI application budget. Enterprise buyers default to purchasing AI from trusted vendors, not building autonomously, because the non-code costs of enterprise software (security certification, compliance, disaster recovery, change management, legal liability) remain prohibitive for DIY approaches throughout the 5-year window. AI agents are used as SaaS tools, not substitutes for them. SaaS absolute spend grows substantially even as its share of a rapidly expanding market holds at or above 60%.

For this scenario to materialize, several conditions must hold simultaneously. Gartner's 40%+ agentic project cancellation rate must materialize as forecast ([https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027)), resetting enterprise confidence in DIY agentic builds. Salesforce's Agentforce must scale to become the dominant AI agentic delivery channel rather than a defensive pricing pivot. AI-generated code quality deficits must remain unresolved at enterprise grade: Veracode's finding that AI-generated code introduces security vulnerabilities in 45% of cases across 100+ LLMs ([https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/](https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/)) and the 9.1% commercial-codebase success rate on SWE-bench Pro ([https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.16941](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.16941)) must not materially improve by 2027. Enterprise buy preference must continue strengthening — or at minimum holding — at the 76% level documented by Menlo Ventures ([https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/)). And SaaS gross retention must hold near 90% with no measurable NRR changes attributable to AI displacement emerging in 2026–2027 earnings ([https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas)).

The bull scenario's strongest advocates are intellectually credible but carry significant conflicts of interest. **Jensen Huang**, CEO of Nvidia, stated on the day of Nvidia's 73% revenue growth quarter: "This notion that the software industry is in decline and being replaced by AI is the most illogical thing in the world"; he frames AI agents as "intelligent software that uses these tools [SAP, ServiceNow, Cadence] on our behalf" rather than replacing them ([https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/26/nvidia-jensen-huang-gpu-ai-threat-software-companies-saas-earnings-chips.html](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/26/nvidia-jensen-huang-gpu-ai-threat-software-companies-saas-earnings-chips.html)). Huang's position has the advantage of relative independence — Nvidia GPU revenue grows regardless of whether enterprises buy SaaS or build custom, making his bull position less financially motivated than most. **Orlando Bravo**, Founder and Managing Partner of Thoma Bravo, who closed $34.4 billion in SaaS buyout funds in 2025 and took Dayforce private for $12.3 billion ([https://www.corpdev.org/2025/06/05/thoma-bravo-secures-34-4-billion-buyout-fund-haul-decoding-the-software-stocks-new-power-play/](https://www.corpdev.org/2025/06/05/thoma-bravo-secures-34-4-billion-buyout-fund-haul-decoding-the-software-stocks-new-power-play/)), argues: "Software is not at all about the code or about the technology — software is about your domain knowledge" and companies with 30 years of domain expertise "are really, really cheap right now" ([https://capwolf.com/orlando-bravo-why-software-stocks-are-oversold-now/](https://capwolf.com/orlando-bravo-why-software-stocks-are-oversold-now/)). Bravo is explicitly talking his book — the largest technology-focused private equity deployment in history — but the domain knowledge argument has independent analytical merit separate from his financial position. **Maribel Lopez**, Principal at Lopez Research, with limited disclosed conflicts of interest, notes that "the reports of SaaS's death are greatly exaggerated" and that the narrative is generating "real confusion for enterprise buyers" who are making suboptimal procurement decisions based on premature displacement assumptions ([https://www.lopezresearch.com/saas-isnt-dead-but-the-dead-narrative-is-leading-enterprise-buyers-astray/](https://www.lopezresearch.com/saas-isnt-dead-but-the-dead-narrative-is-leading-enterprise-buyers-astray/)).

The weakest assumption in the bull scenario is that SaaS incumbents can successfully monetize agentic AI at the pace required to offset seat compression. Only 16% of SaaS incumbents had commercialized AI as a standalone product as of 2025 — though those that have report 2–3x higher customer traction ([https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/upgrading-software-business-models-to-thrive-in-the-ai-era](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/upgrading-software-business-models-to-thrive-in-the-ai-era)). If SaaS vendors defend market share but cannot convert seat revenue into agent revenue fast enough, the bull case on SaaS *dominance* is achieved but the bull case on SaaS *financial health* is not, undermining the category's reinvestment capacity.

Leading indicators that would signal the bull scenario is materializing: Salesforce Q4 FY2027 Agentforce ARR exceeding $5 billion with 80%+ of bookings from existing customer expansion; two or more high-profile enterprise custom-build failures becoming public and resetting CIO risk tolerance; METR task-horizon doubling time extending beyond 250 days. Note: The fourth indicator — no AI coding tool achieving FedRAMP authorization by end of 2027 — has been triggered ahead of schedule: Windsurf achieved FedRAMP authorization in early 2026. The relevant forward-looking indicator is now whether higher-adoption tools (GitHub Copilot Enterprise, Cursor, Claude Code) achieve FedRAMP authorization within 2026–2027, and whether SaaS platform AI features (Agentforce, Now Assist) close the FedRAMP certification lag for their AI-specific feature sets within 12–18 months.

### BASE SCENARIO: Hybrid Dominance with Meaningful SaaS Erosion

**Market Share Range:** 47–58% of enterprise AI application procurement by 2027; 43–55% by 2030
**Probability: 55%**

In this scenario, SaaS retains majority status as the primary procurement modality through 2030, but loses 10–20 percentage points of market share as agentic coding tools cross economic feasibility thresholds for a growing set of bounded enterprise use cases. The displacement is category-selective — concentrated in horizontal point solutions, workflow automation, internal tooling, and mid-market BI — while regulated verticals, platform infrastructure, and mission-critical systems of record remain structurally protected. SaaS vendors simultaneously face pricing model disruption (seat-based to consumption), point-solution displacement (by AI agents and custom builds), and competitive pressure from AI-native startups growing at 400% year-over-year ([https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-state-of-ai-2025](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-state-of-ai-2025)). The net result is a SaaS sector that is smaller as a share of enterprise software spend, but still the dominant procurement modality — a differentiated landscape where the strongest platform incumbents grow while the long tail of point-product SaaS contracts by 25–35%.

The base scenario requires that agentic coding tools continue maturing at their documented trajectory — METR's task-completion time horizon doubling every 88.6–196.5 days ([https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/)) producing agents capable of autonomous multi-day software projects by 2028–2029 — while enterprise-grade quality gaps (45% security vulnerability rate, 9.1% commercial-codebase success on SWE-bench Pro) prevent generalized deployment at scale. SaaS inflation must continue at current rates — 12.2% annually — compressing build-vs-buy break-even timelines to 18–30 months for commodity workflows and driving targeted CFO-level build decisions. Gartner and Deloitte's 35% point-product displacement projection must materialize in the long tail of horizontal, UI-first, CRUD-adjacent tools while core systems of record remain intact. And enterprise governance maturity must improve from the current 21% baseline ([https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html)) to roughly 45–55% by 2029, enabling larger-scale build deployments in the 2028–2030 window.

The base scenario's most credible advocates span independent analysts and enterprise practitioners, supported by broader institutional data from the major consulting firms. **Bo Lykkegaard**, Associate VP for Software Research Europe at IDC, provides the clearest institutional framing: "SaaS is being disrupted, not by decline but by evolution"; the per-seat model will be obsolete by 2028, but the SaaS category survives ([https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/is-saas-dead-rethinking-the-future-of-software-in-the-age-of-ai/](https://www.idc.com/resource-cache/blog/is-saas-dead-rethinking-the-future-of-software-in-the-age-of-ai/)). **Ayo Odusote**, Software and Platforms Leader at Deloitte, writes that "many enterprises will ultimately balance both approaches rather than choosing one exclusively"; the market is "becoming more hybrid" and vendors must "earn renewal, not extract it" ([https://www.cio.com/article/4131904/saas-isnt-dead-the-market-is-just-becoming-more-hybrid.html](https://www.cio.com/article/4131904/saas-isnt-dead-the-market-is-just-becoming-more-hybrid.html)). BCG and EY-Parthenon similarly land on transformation, not extinction. The institutional consulting data — from these analysts and their colleagues at McKinsey, Bain, Gartner, and Forrester — is the most coordinated agreement across the entire 9-wave corpus: transformation, not extinction, is the modal outcome.

**Category-Dependent Analysts — Strengthening the BASE Scenario's Structural Foundation**

The analytically most rigorous frameworks in the entire research corpus are not simple bull/base/bear calls — they are category-dependent predictions that directly reinforce the BASE scenario's structural architecture. Five major institutional analysts converge on this framework:

**Chuck Whitten** (Bain & Company) predicts a "heterogeneous landscape where AI and SaaS complement and compete in various ways" — explicitly naming compliance/platform SaaS as durable while identifying horizontal point-solutions (HubSpot list-building, Monday.com task boards) as AI-feasible substitution targets ([https://www.bain.com/insights/the-great-debate-will-agentic-ai-kill-saas/](https://www.bain.com/insights/the-great-debate-will-agentic-ai-kill-saas/)). **Denis Torii** (Gartner VP Analyst) predicts enterprises will "Buy, Build, Blend" — the only Gartner analyst to directly engage with buy-vs-build as a framework, and one who explicitly rejects the binary outcome ([https://www.agilepoint.com/blog-posts/future-of-enterprise-application](https://www.agilepoint.com/blog-posts/future-of-enterprise-application)). **Kate Leggett and Charles Betz** (both Forrester VP Principal Analysts) predict enterprise-core and compliance-constrained SaaS is durable while horizontal point-solutions face structural exposure — the body text of Leggett's widely-cited "SaaS As We Know It Is Dead" report is, despite its headline, a category-differentiation argument that projects SaaS spending at $576B by 2029 ([https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/)). **Jeremy Schneider** (McKinsey Senior Partner) publishes a Make-or-Buy Matrix with an explicit category logic: build where genuine competitive differentiation exists, buy where speed, cost control, or proven reliability matter more — a principled conditional that defaults toward buy for most enterprise capabilities ([https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-ai-centric-imperative-navigating-the-next-software-frontier](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-ai-centric-imperative-navigating-the-next-software-frontier)). **Ryan Hinkle** (Insight Partners Managing Director) offers the investor community's clearest category split: "filing cabinet" SaaS (data storage with low workflow dependency) is vulnerable to displacement while "systems of action" (mission-critical, workflow-embedded) will retain buyers ([https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/2026-investor-predictions/](https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/2026-investor-predictions/)).

The convergence of McKinsey, Gartner, Bain, Forrester (x2), and Insight Partners on category-dependent outcomes — rather than any single directional call — is the strongest institutional signal in the corpus. It is not a prediction that SaaS wins or loses; it is a structural prediction that the answer depends on which software category is in question. This category-differentiation framework directly produces the BASE scenario's most important insight: SaaS retains majority aggregate status because the low-risk categories (platform SaaS, regulated vertical SaaS, financial systems, security SaaS) represent the majority of enterprise SaaS expenditure — even as the high-risk categories (workflow automation, standalone BI, mid-market CRM, internal tooling) face bounded but real displacement. No major analyst house predicts that custom build will become the majority enterprise procurement method across all software categories within the 5-year window.

This scenario is also supported by the most credible independent technical voice in the corpus. **Andrej Karpathy**, co-founder of OpenAI and independent researcher, describes the 2025–2035 decade as the "Decade of the Agents" — a transition in which the engineering profession is being "dramatically refactored" — while noting that full human-replacement agents are "about a decade away" and that agents fail on complex and rare codebases in ways that limit near-term enterprise deployment ([https://www.latent.space/p/s3](https://www.latent.space/p/s3)). Karpathy's position is notable for its independence: he carries no financial stake in either SaaS durability or SaaS displacement.

The weakest assumption in the base scenario is the maintenance cost trajectory for AI-built software. Wave 9's TCO analysis identifies the annual maintenance cost rate — how much it costs to maintain AI-generated enterprise software year-over-year relative to the initial build cost — as the single highest-leverage variable in the entire break-even model. Classical software maintenance costs 15–20% of initial build cost annually. The base scenario assumes partial automation reduces this to 8–12% by 2028. If agentic tools reduce it further, to 1–5%, the base case economics shift materially toward the bear case.

Leading indicators that would confirm the base scenario: 2027 earnings showing median SaaS NRR dropping from ~108% to 100–105% range (first financial evidence of seat compression without full vendor replacement); Zylo and Torii data showing gradual reduction in average enterprise SaaS application portfolio from the current 106-app benchmark; Goldman Sachs and Jefferies "AI loser" sell ratings on DocuSign and Atlassian proving accurate in revenue deceleration (not just equity repricing); and EU Data Act exit-fee ban (effective January 2027) triggering measurable but bounded switching in European enterprise portfolios.

### BEAR SCENARIO: Structural SaaS Displacement

**Market Share Range:** 30–45% of enterprise AI application procurement by 2027; 25–40% by 2030
**Probability: 22%** (revised from 20% following Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026)

In this scenario, agentic coding tools cross the enterprise-grade quality threshold by 2027–2028, fundamentally collapsing the economic case for purchasing software in the majority of workflow-layer and mid-market categories. Combined with AI inference cost deflation — OpenAI o3 inference costs dropped 80% in two months per Bain ([https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/)) — SaaS pricing-as-extraction reaching its political limit with CFOs, and the emergence of superhuman coder AI capability eliminating the talent constraint on building, enterprises accelerate replacement of the majority of their SaaS point-product estates. Platform SaaS survives as infrastructure but is redefined as an API substrate rather than a user-facing application, collapsing ACV. SaaS share drops below 45% by 2028 and continues declining toward 30–35% by 2030.

The bear scenario requires conditions that current evidence does not support for the 5-year window. METR's task-horizon doubling at the 88.6-day rate must persist through 2027, producing agents capable of autonomous multi-week software projects by late 2027 — the fastest end of its documented range ([https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/)). AI-generated code security deficits must fall from 45% to below 10% by 2027 — a target that Veracode's research found flat across model sizes as of 2025 ([https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/](https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/)). Maintenance costs must compress to 1–5% of initial build annually. Enterprise governance must triple from 21% to 60%+ in three years. And SaaS pricing inflation must continue at 12.2% or accelerate, pushing CFO-mandated build programs beyond the current early-adopter population. All of these conditions must hold simultaneously. The bear scenario's 20% probability reflects the genuine and rising tail risk that the capability trajectory arrives faster than the institutional consensus expects — not a dismissal of the scenario.

The bear scenario's most credible named advocates are primarily equity analysts with formal rating actions and build-platform founders with direct financial stakes — a conflict worth acknowledging explicitly while not dismissing the substance of their positions. **Jackson Ader**, Lead Software Analyst at KeyBanc, issued a rare Underweight rating on ServiceNow in January 2026, explicitly citing "seat count pressure" and "worrying trends in IT back-office employment data" — the most direct procurement-direction statement from any equity analyst in the corpus ([https://finance.yahoo.com/news/keybanc-downgrades-servicenow-says-seat-count-pressure-ai-growth-concerns-create-downside-risk-150004889.html](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/keybanc-downgrades-servicenow-says-seat-count-pressure-ai-growth-concerns-create-downside-risk-150004889.html)). **Brent Thill**, Lead Software Analyst at Jefferies, called a full sector "reset" in his February 23, 2026 note and formally downgraded Workday and DocuSign with explicit AI disruption attribution: "Seat-based licensing faces structural disruption" ([https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/workday-stock-ai-threat-downgrade-jefferies-2026-2](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/workday-stock-ai-threat-downgrade-jefferies-2026-2)). These two equity analysts provide the most credible bear-camp confirmation: formal investment recommendations with explicit procurement-direction reasoning and sell-side accountability.

**Bill Vass**, CTO of Booz Allen Hamilton — a government software builder whose revenue expands with government custom-build activity — states: "Agentic systems can now read your business policies and generate the software. This is going to disintermediate these SaaS vendors and these ERP vendors" ([https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas)). **Chamath Palihapitiya**, founder of Social Capital and the 8090 incubator — which is explicitly built to replace enterprise SaaS at a 90% discount — is the most direct commercial bet on the bear thesis: "Tell us what enterprise software you use and my team and I will build you an 80% feature-complete version at a 90% discount."

**Note on two commonly cited bear voices:** Satya Nadella's widely-quoted statement that "business applications will probably all collapse in the Agent Era" is better classified as NUANCED-CONDITIONAL rather than bear: his proposed replacement (Copilot Studio, Agent 365, Dynamics) is still procured from Microsoft — his thesis is that enterprises stop buying from Salesforce/Workday and start buying from Microsoft, not that they stop buying. Sam Altman's "service as software" framing describes an AI delivery model, not a procurement-modality shift — enterprises could satisfy "service as software" by purchasing AI-powered SaaS rather than building custom tools. Neither Nadella nor Altman made direct statements about enterprise buy-vs-build procurement and neither should be cited as bear-camp evidence for SaaS displacement by custom builds.

The weakest assumption in the bear case is the maintenance cost automation claim. The bear scenario requires that agentic tools not only cut initial build costs — which is already demonstrated for bounded, greenfield tasks — but that they also dramatically reduce *ongoing* maintenance costs, a claim supported only by one RPA-context analysis (Duvo AI) and uncontrolled vendor reports, not validated in broad enterprise software contexts ([https://blog.duvo.ai/why-every-rpa-project-breaks-and-how-agentic-ai-fixes-it](https://blog.duvo.ai/why-every-rpa-project-breaks-and-how-agentic-ai-fixes-it)). A 50% reduction in initial build cost saves $500,000 one-time on a $1 million build. A reduction in the 20% annual maintenance rate to 1% saves $190,000 per year in perpetuity, exceeding the initial saving by year 3 and compounding indefinitely. The bear case does not close economically until this maintenance claim is validated.

Leading indicators that would signal the bear scenario is materializing: METR task-horizon hitting 8+ hour autonomous tasks by end of 2026; any Tier 1 AI coding tool achieving FedRAMP authorization [FIRED: Windsurf achieved FedRAMP authorization in early 2026; the next-magnitude trigger is GitHub Copilot Enterprise or Cursor authorization, which would warrant a further 5–7pp bear revision]; two or more Fortune 50 enterprises publicly announcing wholesale SaaS replacement programs with documented $100M+ spend reductions; SaaS median NRR falling below 95% across public market names in 2026–2027 earnings; and Goldman Sachs' named "AI losers" basket reporting confirmed revenue deceleration, not cyclical correction.

## Part IV: The Central Tension — What This Debate Is Actually About

### Business Model Disruption vs. Architectural Disruption

The 9-wave research corpus circles an irreducible question that the loudest participants in the "SaaS is dead" debate rarely state explicitly: is the enterprise software market undergoing a *business model disruption* — which SaaS as a procurement category will absorb and survive — or an *architectural disruption* — which will eventually hollow out SaaS's reason to exist? These are structurally different claims that require different evidence and produce different probability estimates, but participants on both sides frequently deploy evidence from one type of disruption to argue for conclusions applicable only to the other.

The evidence on business model disruption is robust and multi-wave corroborated. Per-seat pricing is dying — Forrester documents the mechanism, IDC projects the timeline, and the major incumbents are demonstrating survival by shifting to consumption-based and outcome-based models ([https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/)). Historical precedent consistently supports the absorption thesis: cloud computing took 13+ years to achieve majority displacement of on-premise software; open source took more than 10 years to displace commercial software in its target categories; mobile did not kill desktop. Technology revolutions at the enterprise layer are rarely binary, as Bain's Chuck Whitten observes — they create ecosystems that are a mix of old and new, differentiated by category. If the debate is about pricing model disruption, the hypothesis is confirmed with high confidence.

The evidence on architectural disruption is directional, less corroborated, but structurally serious in a different way. Satya Nadella's argument that business applications are "essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic" that AI agents will manage without a SaaS intermediary ([https://www.windowscentral.com/microsoft/hey-why-do-i-need-excel-microsoft-ceo-satya-nadella-foresees-a-disruptive-agentic-ai-era-that-could-aggressively-collapse-saas-apps](https://www.windowscentral.com/microsoft/hey-why-do-i-need-excel-microsoft-ceo-satya-nadella-foresees-a-disruptive-agentic-ai-era-that-could-aggressively-collapse-saas-apps)) — combined with McKinsey's "post-SaaS architecture" in which a human employee primarily interacts with one agent interface while back-end agents and APIs act directly on data repositories ([https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/upgrading-software-business-models-to-thrive-in-the-ai-era](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/upgrading-software-business-models-to-thrive-in-the-ai-era)) — describes a world where the SaaS application layer is genuinely disintermediated. If this is the correct frame, the question of which pricing model wins becomes secondary to whether any independent software application survives as an addressable market.

### Why Participants Talk Past Each Other

The discourse is systematically confused because participants operate with different definitions of "SaaS," different enterprise segments in mind, and different time horizons. When Salesforce's Marc Benioff dismisses the "SaaSpocalypse" narrative, he is reasoning from Tier 1 platform SaaS with deep data integration and 200% Agentforce ARR growth — a set of facts that genuinely supports bull-case conclusions. When Replit's Amjad Masad describes building a $400 custom application to replace a $150,000 SaaS subscription ([https://venturebeat.com/ai/for-replits-ceo-the-future-of-software-is-agents-all-the-way-down](https://venturebeat.com/ai/for-replits-ceo-the-future-of-software-is-agents-all-the-way-down)), he is reasoning from a narrow, greenfield internal tool — a set of facts that genuinely supports bear-case conclusions. Both are describing real phenomena in non-overlapping segments. When enterprise analysts cite the METR productivity RCT showing 19% slowdown on complex mature codebases ([https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/)), they are measuring a different population (experienced developers on enterprise legacy code) than vendor-reported outcomes measuring early adopters on greenfield tasks. The claims are not contradictory; they apply to different contexts.

The midmarket enterprise vs. Fortune 500 distinction compounds this. The Menlo Ventures survey capturing 76% buy-over-build preference ([https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/)) and the Retool survey capturing 35% replacement rates ([https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026)) are not measuring the same population. The former captures the AI *infrastructure* procurement decision — enterprises buying LLMs and AI platforms rather than building them. The latter captures the *individual software category* replacement decision in a sample biased toward technical builders. These are complementary findings: you can simultaneously be buying 76% of your AI infrastructure *and* building custom tools to replace some SaaS subscriptions. The surveys are orthogonal, not contradictory, but market observers routinely deploy them as if they were measuring the same thing.

### The Synthesis Resolution

The most analytically defensible resolution of the central tension is that both framings are *simultaneously operative* in different segments of the SaaS market. The business model disruption frame is correct for Tier 1 platform SaaS (Salesforce, ServiceNow, SAP, Microsoft) and regulated vertical SaaS (Epic, Veeva, Guidewire) — these companies will survive by transforming. The architectural disruption frame is increasingly correct for the point-solution long tail — the 35% of point-product SaaS tools Gartner and Deloitte project to be replaced by 2030. The central tension is therefore not a binary but a *boundary-drawing problem*: where exactly does the line run between SaaS categories that will absorb the disruption and SaaS categories that will be architecturally disintermediated — and how far does that line move within the 2–5 year window?

The evidence converges on the answer that, within 2–5 years, the line runs at approximately the boundary between platform SaaS and regulated vertical SaaS on one side (durable) and horizontal point-solution SaaS and workflow tool SaaS on the other (structurally exposed). Within 2–5 years, that line will move modestly but not dramatically — constrained by enterprise governance immaturity, AI code quality deficits, maintenance cost uncertainty, and the compliance certification cycles that protect the regulated categories. Beyond 5 years, the evidence is genuinely less certain, and the line could move substantially if the METR capability trajectory continues and maintenance automation advances.

## Part V: Conditions, Variables, and Leading Indicators

### The Five Highest-Leverage Variables

The scenario probabilities above are not fixed — they are functions of five quantifiable variables whose trajectories will determine which scenario materializes. Understanding these variables and monitoring their leading indicators is more analytically productive than debating current market share.

**Variable 1: Annual Maintenance Cost of AI-Built Software**

This is, per Wave 9's TCO sensitivity analysis, the single highest-leverage variable in the entire build-vs-buy model — more impactful on 5-year total cost of ownership than initial build cost compression. Classical software maintenance costs 15–20% of initial build cost annually. If agentic tools reduce this to 5%, the break-even calculation shifts dramatically in favor of building. If it stays at 15–20% because AI-generated code creates technical debt faster than agentic maintenance can clear it, the economics remain unfavorable for building complex enterprise software.

Current evidence: The sole primary source addressing this directly — a Duvo AI analysis in the RPA context — is vendor-produced and methodologically opaque ([https://blog.duvo.ai/why-every-rpa-project-breaks-and-how-agentic-ai-fixes-it](https://blog.duvo.ai/why-every-rpa-project-breaks-and-how-agentic-ai-fixes-it)). METR's finding that experienced developers took 19% longer on complex mature codebases ([https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/)) suggests that AI tools may *increase* maintenance complexity on legacy code, not reduce it. No independent large-sample study of maintenance costs for AI-built enterprise software currently exists.

Threshold for scenario shift: Independent evidence that AI-assisted maintenance reduces the effective annual rate below 8% would materially increase bear case probability. Evidence that AI-generated code increases technical debt and sustains maintenance costs above 15% would materially increase bull case probability. The METR productivity RCT model, replicated in a maintenance context, would be the highest-value research contribution to this question.

Leading indicators to track: Enterprise software maintenance cost data from CIO surveys (IDC, Gartner) that specifically separate AI-generated from human-generated code portfolios; public post-mortems from enterprises that built custom agentic tools in 2024–2025 documenting year-two maintenance costs; academic or independent replication of METR's RCT in a maintenance context.

**Variable 2: METR Task-Horizon Trajectory**

METR's research documents AI task-completion time doubling every 88.6 days at the accelerated rate or 196.5 days at the historical rate ([https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/)). At the accelerated rate, agents capable of 8-hour autonomous tasks — the enterprise workday threshold — arrive by late 2026 to early 2027. At the historical rate, they arrive by 2028–2029. This is the most consequential technical variable in the research: it determines whether the bear case timeline fits inside or outside the 5-year window.

Current evidence: The doubling rate has been empirically measured at 88.6 days in METR's most recent update. Geoffrey Hinton, independent of financial conflict, endorses the trajectory. Yann LeCun, Chief AI Scientist at Meta and also independent of financial conflict, argues that current LLM architectures have fundamental limitations in world understanding, persistent memory, reasoning, and planning that constrain this trajectory ([https://www.zdnet.com/article/meta-chief-ai-scientist-llms-not-path-to-human-like-intelligence/](https://www.zdnet.com/article/meta-chief-ai-scientist-llms-not-path-to-human-like-intelligence/)). Gary Marcus's mathematical reliability argument adds a complementary constraint: a 20-step agentic workflow at 95% per-step accuracy has only a 36% probability of full success — far below enterprise requirements of 99.9%+.

Threshold for scenario shift: A verified plateau in METR task-horizon improvement over two consecutive measurement periods (doubling time extending beyond 250 days) would substantially reduce bear case probability and extend the SaaS durability window. Continued doubling at the 88.6-day rate through Q3 2026 would substantially increase bear case probability.

Leading indicators to track: METR's quarterly task-horizon updates (published publicly); SWE-bench Pro commercial codebase success rates (currently 9.1%); Aider's agentic coding progress reports; performance on multi-step enterprise simulation benchmarks (GAIA, MIRAGE).

**Variable 3: Enterprise Governance Maturity for Autonomous Agents**

Only 21% of enterprises currently have mature governance models for autonomous agents per Deloitte (n=3,235) ([https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html)). The base scenario requires this to grow to 45–55% by 2029. The bear scenario requires it to reach 60%+ by 2028. The bull scenario projects it staying below 35% through 2030. Governance is the organizational rate-limiter on the build scenario: even enterprises with sufficient engineering talent cannot deploy custom-built agentic software at scale without frameworks for managing AI errors, human oversight thresholds, liability assignment, and audit trails.

Current evidence: 42% of AI initiatives are abandoned before production per S&P Global, up from 17% in 2024 ([https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/ai-experiences-rapid-adoption-but-with-mixed-outcomes-highlights-from-vote-ai-machine-learning](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/ai-experiences-rapid-adoption-but-with-mixed-outcomes-highlights-from-vote-ai-machine-learning)), which could be interpreted either as the governance bottleneck holding or as the acceleration of the governance learning curve. The EU AI Act's progressive enforcement through 2026 and 2027 creates both governance infrastructure (compliance requirements force documentation) and governance cost (added oversight overhead).

Threshold for scenario shift: The Deloitte governance maturity metric rising from 21% to 40%+ in the 2026 annual report would signal governance bottleneck clearing faster than the base case projects and would increase bear case probability. Staying below 30% in 2026 would confirm the bull case's near-term protection.

Leading indicators to track: Deloitte State of AI annual report (n=3,235+) governance maturity percentage; Gartner agentic AI maturity model placement (currently at Peak of Inflated Expectations); number of enterprises publicly announcing formal agentic AI governance frameworks; NIST AI Risk Management Framework adoption rates.

**Variable 4: AI-Generated Code Security Quality**

Veracode's study across 100+ LLMs and 80 tasks finds AI-generated code introduces security vulnerabilities in 45% of cases — flat across model sizes ([https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/](https://www.veracode.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-genai-code-security-report/)). CodeRabbit finds AI-generated pull requests contain 2.74x more XSS vulnerabilities than human-written code. Cloud Security Alliance finds 62% of AI-generated code has design flaws or known vulnerabilities. This security deficit is the most concrete quality barrier to enterprise custom-build deployment: enterprise security teams cannot approve production deployment of code with a 45% vulnerability rate without prohibitive remediation overhead. Windsurf (formerly Codeium) achieved FedRAMP authorization in early 2026, becoming the first major agentic coding assistant certified for U.S. federal and regulated enterprise use ([https://www.fedramp.gov/ai/](https://www.fedramp.gov/ai/)). This resolves the development-tool access barrier for FedRAMP environments but does not address the code-quality security deficit: FedRAMP certifies the tool's operational security posture, not the security of the code it produces. The Veracode 45% vulnerability rate and Cloud Security Alliance 62% design-flaw finding remain the primary evidence points for Variable 4.

Current evidence: Veracode's finding is unusually robust — multi-LLM, multi-task methodology, finding that vulnerability rates are flat across model sizes removes the hope that simply scaling models will solve the problem. The Cloud Security Alliance corroborates the direction. This variable currently supports the bull and base scenarios.

Threshold for scenario shift: A 2026 Veracode update showing vulnerability rates falling below 25% across top models would signal meaningful quality improvement and shift scenario probability toward the base or bear. Any Tier 1 model's FedRAMP authorization was identified as a leading indicator for the bear case in regulated sectors. Windsurf achieved FedRAMP authorization in early 2026 — a partial trigger of this indicator. Windsurf is not the largest agentic coding tool by adoption (that position belongs to GitHub Copilot Enterprise), but its authorization establishes the institutional precedent. GitHub Copilot Enterprise or Claude Code achieving FedRAMP authorization would constitute the full trigger of this indicator and would warrant a 5–7pp bear-case revision.

Leading indicators to track: Veracode annual AI code security reports (primary indicator — 45% vulnerability rate is the binding constraint); FedRAMP marketplace AI/agentic tool listings — Windsurf now listed (early 2026); whether GitHub Copilot Enterprise, Cursor, or Claude Code achieve FedRAMP authorization within 2026–2027 (higher-magnitude trigger than Windsurf authorization); whether SaaS platform AI features (Salesforce Agentforce, ServiceNow Now Assist) obtain FedRAMP ATO coverage, closing the certification-lag window described in Part V; NIST cybersecurity AI framework guideline updates; enterprise CISO survey data on AI code deployment approval rates.

**Variable 5: SaaS Incumbent Pricing Discipline**

Salesforce extracted 72% of its 2025 ARR growth from price increases rather than new customers ([https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/](https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/)). SaaS inflation at 12.2% annually means a $55.7M average enterprise SaaS spend grows to $73M over three years without additional procurement decisions. The EU Data Act's ban on exit fees effective January 2027 will reduce vendor lock-in for European enterprises. 60% of vendors are masking price increases through AI feature bundling ([https://www.vertice.one/l/saas-inflation-index-report](https://www.vertice.one/l/saas-inflation-index-report)). CFO-level response to continued pricing extraction is the primary commercial driver of build mandates — the mechanism by which SaaS pricing behavior converts into build behavior.

Current evidence: Price extraction is continuing at high rates with limited enterprise resistance in aggregate spend data, though Zylo's 52.7% unused license figure ([https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/](https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/)) suggests growing procurement attention. The EU Data Act represents the first major regulatory intervention in vendor lock-in.

Threshold for scenario shift: SaaS inflation deceleration to below 8% annually would reduce build incentive and support the bull case. Acceleration above 15% or multiple high-profile price disputes becoming public litigation would support the base-to-bear transition.

Leading indicators to track: Vertice quarterly SaaS Inflation Index; SaaStr vendor price increase announcements; EU Data Act exit-fee ban enforcement actions starting January 2027; CFO survey data on SaaS cost management priorities (Gartner, IDC annual surveys).

### The FedRAMP Certification-Lag Window

Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026 created a specific asymmetric competitive dynamic for major SaaS platforms with federal customer bases. Salesforce Government Cloud and ServiceNow FedRAMP are already authorized for their core platforms, but their AI features — Agentforce, Now Assist — are not necessarily covered under existing ATOs. The typical lag between a new product capability and its inclusion in a FedRAMP ATO is 12–24 months, during which federal buyers cannot use the AI feature in FedRAMP environments.

This creates a bounded time window in which:
- Federal agencies can use Windsurf (FedRAMP authorized) to build custom AI-assisted tools
- They may not be able to use Agentforce or Now Assist AI features in their FedRAMP environments if those specific features lack ATO coverage
- The competitive equation tips temporarily toward build-over-buy in the federal market

This is not a permanent structural shift — Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Microsoft have both the resources and the incentive to close this gap rapidly. But it creates urgency. The size and duration of this window will be determined by how quickly SaaS platform vendors pursue FedRAMP certification for their AI feature sets specifically, not just their core platforms. If Salesforce can certify Agentforce under FedRAMP within 6 months, the window is narrow. If it takes 24 months, the window is substantial and the build-vs-buy calculus in federal environments shifts meaningfully for that period.

The most sophisticated regulated-sector enterprises — federal agencies, defense contractors, major regulated banks — will likely run parallel tracks: piloting Windsurf-assisted custom builds for bounded internal tooling while pressing SaaS vendors to accelerate FedRAMP certification of their AI features. This behavior is consistent with the Capital One pattern documented in the original research — building proprietary agentic stacks alongside maintained vendor relationships.

**Leading indicator to watch:** SaaS platform vendor announcements of FedRAMP ATO expansions to cover AI features (Agentforce, Now Assist, M365 Copilot in GCC). This is the event that closes the certification-lag window. If these announcements arrive within 6 months, the competitive window is a minor footnote. If they do not arrive within 18 months, this dynamic will generate observable procurement behavior in federal IT budgets.

## Part VI: The Verdict

### 1. Is SaaS "Dead" in 2–5 Years?

No, at approximately 95%+ probability. The word "dead" — used loosely in the "SaaSpocalypse" discourse — would require SaaS to cease being the majority procurement modality and experience absolute revenue decline. No evidence supports this. Forrester projects SaaS spending at $576 billion by 2029 ([https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/)). Gartner documents 14.7% software spending growth in 2026 ([https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/](https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/)). The equity selloff of February 2026 is a sentiment event with no confirming revenue data — gross retention in enterprise SaaS holds near 90%, no hard NRR changes attributable to AI displacement appear in Q3/Q4 2025 or Q1 2026 earnings, and software sector ROIC of 50.17% vastly exceeds WACC of 9.34%. The per-seat pricing model will be dead as the dominant model by 2028 — IDC's 70% vendor refactoring projection is credible and well-corroborated. But pricing model death and category death are analytically distinct. The Forrester headline "SaaS As We Know It Is Dead" is accurate as a headline about the pricing model and misleading as a claim about the procurement category — its own body text, projecting $576 billion in spending by 2029, demonstrates the distinction.

### 2. Is SaaS the Majority Procurement Method in 2–5 Years?

Yes, at approximately **85% probability by 2027** (bull 33% + base 52%) and **75% probability by 2030** (bull 18% + base 57%), revised from 87%/77% following Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026. The revision reflects a 2pp reduction in bull probability due to partial invalidation of the compliance moat argument, with corresponding increase in bear probability. Both revised figures remain within the original ±8–10 percentage point confidence interval. The confidence interval is wider for 2030 because the technical trajectory uncertainty — specifically the METR task-horizon doubling rate and maintenance cost automation — is genuinely unresolved, and the 2028–2030 window is where the bear case has its highest probability mass. The 77% figure for 2030 should be read as: in roughly one in four probability-weighted outcomes, SaaS will have fallen below majority status by 2030, primarily because horizontal point-solution SaaS displacement — which is already occurring — compounds with improving agentic capability and enterprise governance maturity to cross the threshold in the most displacement-exposed categories.

The qualification is that "majority" does not mean "unchanged." The base case — the most probable single scenario at 55% — projects SaaS share declining from its current ~65–70% to 43–55% by 2030. That is a meaningful contraction in relative terms, concentrated in the categories identified in Part III (workflow automation, standalone BI, mid-market CRM, internal tooling). The largest and most valuable SaaS categories remain durable. SaaS retains majority status, but the composition of that majority shifts: platform SaaS and regulated vertical SaaS become a larger proportion of the SaaS total as the point-product long tail erodes.

### 3. What Does the Original Hypothesis Get Right?

The hypothesis correctly identifies four things. First, it correctly predicts the 2–5 year outcome: enterprise inertia, compliance moats, organizational governance immaturity, and the genuine difficulty of maintaining custom-built software at enterprise scale will collectively prevent SaaS from falling below majority status in the near term. Second, it correctly identifies the direction of incumbent adaptation: the major SaaS platforms (Salesforce, ServiceNow) are successfully embedding AI rather than being displaced by it, and their successful adaptation is a real phenomenon with hard financial evidence in audited earnings. Third, it correctly anticipates that the agentic coding narrative is ahead of enterprise deployment reality: Gartner's 40%+ cancellation forecast and BCG's 5% achieving-at-scale finding confirm the execution gap the hypothesis implies. Fourth, it correctly frames the question as one about enterprise behavior (procurement method), not technological capability — which is the right level of analysis.

### 4. What Does the Original Hypothesis Miss or Understate?

The hypothesis understates or misses four important things. First, it does not adequately distinguish between the *aggregate* market and the *category-level* reality. The hypothesis is confirmed in aggregate but conceals that specific SaaS categories — workflow automation, standalone analytics, internal tooling — are already experiencing displacement that will reach 35% of the point-product market by 2030 per Gartner and Deloitte. An enterprise SaaS portfolio is not a monolith: some of it is as durable as Epic's EHR and some of it is as vulnerable as a mid-market dashboard tool.

Second, the hypothesis conflates pricing model durability with procurement category durability. The per-seat model is not surviving — all nine waves confirm this with independent evidence. The hypothesis could be read to imply that SaaS pricing is stable; it is not. What is stable is the *buy-over-build preference* for the categories where SaaS has genuine moats. The pricing transformation is real, significant, and will compress vendor margins even in categories where procurement share is defended.

Third, the hypothesis underweights the 2028–2030 tail risk. The 5-year verdict (77% probability) is meaningfully less confident than the 2-year verdict (87% probability) because the technical capability trajectory and organizational governance maturity are both moving in directions that favor displacement, and they are likely to compound in the 2028–2030 window. The original hypothesis implies a static analysis; the research reveals a dynamic one with a specific inflection window.

Fourth, the hypothesis does not account for segment heterogeneity. The hypothesis is most accurately confirmed for regulated industries (healthcare, financial services), mid-market enterprises (lacking engineering depth), and SMBs (no engineering capacity). One qualification to the regulated-industry durability claim: Windsurf's FedRAMP authorization in early 2026 demonstrates that the compliance-tool-access barrier for federal and regulated enterprises is surmountable. The regulated-sector moat remains high through a combination of data gravity, domain expertise, and the ATO requirement for deployed software — but it is no longer resting on an unbreached compliance wall for development tooling. The hypothesis is still most confidently confirmed for regulated industries; the qualification is that this confidence is no longer based partly on an absolute compliance gate. It is least confidently confirmed for Fortune 500 tech-native enterprises (Shopify, Stripe, Coinbase) which have the talent, governance infrastructure, and risk tolerance to lead SaaS displacement in the 2027–2030 window. The aggregate majority is real; it conceals bear-case outcomes in the segments best positioned to build.

### 5. Best Single Probability Estimate

**"SaaS remains the majority enterprise procurement method by 2028 (3 years out):" ~83% probability.**

**"SaaS remains the majority enterprise procurement method by 2030 (5 years out):" ~77% probability.**

**Note on expert consensus quality:** A post-publication audit of the 164 named experts in the underlying L2 registry found that approximately 50 experts originally assigned to bull/base/bear camps made no direct statement about enterprise SaaS procurement vs. custom build — their statements addressed AI capability trajectories, coding productivity, spending volume, pricing model evolution, or VC investment strategy. The audit moved these experts to an UNCLEAR category and added a CATEGORY-DEPENDENT category for the eight highest-quality analytical frameworks (McKinsey/Schneider, Gartner/Torii, Bain/Whitten, Forrester/Leggett and Betz, Insight Partners/Hinkle, a16z/Rodriguez, ServiceNow/Zavery) whose positions are explicitly that outcomes vary by software category. This revision strengthens, not weakens, the BASE scenario: the most analytically rigorous institutional frameworks in the corpus converge on category-dependent heterogeneous outcomes — exactly the structure the BASE scenario describes. The genuine bear camp (after removing capability statements, pricing-model statements, and competitive-positioning statements) is smaller and more specifically composed of equity analysts with formal rating actions (Ader, Thill) and build-platform founders with direct commercial stakes (Masad, Palihapitiya, Hsu) — a credible but concentrated group, not a broad institutional consensus.

These estimates are grounded in the scenario architecture developed across 9 research waves and 164 named experts. They should be understood as probability estimates over a well-defined outcome (SaaS retaining greater than 50% of enterprise software application procurement spend as measured by Gartner's enterprise application software category), not as predictions of specific market share levels. The confidence interval around these estimates is approximately ±8–10 percentage points, reflecting genuine uncertainty about the METR capability trajectory, the maintenance cost question, and enterprise governance maturity pace — all of which are currently unresolved in the primary evidence.

The most important single sentence that summarizes the research: **SaaS is not dying — it is bifurcating.** Platform SaaS and regulated vertical SaaS are gaining AI capability and deepening moats; horizontal point-solution SaaS is losing relevance at the margins at a rate that will compound through 2030. The "SaaS is dead" narrative conflates the bifurcation with extinction. The research evidence does not support extinction within 5 years, but does support meaningful and accelerating erosion in the most exposed categories. The original hypothesis is confirmed — with the qualification that the enterprise software landscape it describes is not static, and the next 5 years will be defined by which side of the bifurcation each SaaS category lands on.
