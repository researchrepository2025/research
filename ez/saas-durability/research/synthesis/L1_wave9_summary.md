## 1. Wave Theme

Wave 9's TCO and economic analysis reveals a structural paradox: SaaS has never been more expensive to own, and custom software has never been cheaper to build — but neither statement determines which model wins. The fully-loaded enterprise SaaS TCO runs 1.7x to 3.6x the headline license fee when implementation, integration, training, administration, compliance, and vendor management are included, and SaaS inflation is running at 12.2% annually versus 2.7% general CPI — a 4.5x gap that compounds against locked-in customers year over year. At the same time, agentic coding tools have compressed initial development costs by a vendor-claimed 50–95%, and a live survey of 817 enterprise builders finds 35% have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom build. Yet the economics are deeply category-dependent and time-horizon-sensitive. ERP and security/compliance remain structurally in SaaS territory; internal tools have already crossed the TCO break-even threshold. The highest-leverage variable governing which model wins is not initial build cost — where agentic compression is loudest — but annual maintenance cost rate and user-count trajectory, both of which favor SaaS incumbency in regulated, high-complexity workflows and favor custom build in commodity, workflow-layer, and internal tooling applications. On purely financial grounds, the wave supports a base case in which SaaS retains majority share but suffers material erosion in the 25–35% range of the point-product market by 2030, with the bear and bull cases bounded by the actual trajectory of agentic maintenance cost reduction — the single variable with the highest leverage on the outcome.

## 2. Key Evidence for Each Scenario

### Bull Case (SaaS remains dominant, 60%+ market share in 5 years)

- [Large IT projects run 45% over budget, deliver 56% less value than predicted, and only 1 in 200 delivers on time and within budget](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/delivering-large-scale-it-projects-on-time-on-budget-and-on-value) — the baseline failure rate for custom builds creates a persistent risk premium that favors buying for most enterprise decision-makers.
- [Gartner found that organizations overlook 50–70% of total ownership costs in software calculations, and SaaS has a lower TCO in its first two years](https://sandis.io/resources/tco-custom-software-vs-saas-platforms) — the majority of CFOs using 2–3 year payback periods will continue to favor SaaS on NPV grounds.
- [ERP implementations carry a 55–75% failure rate and cost overruns averaging 215%, making custom build "economically irrational for most enterprises"](https://godlan.com/erp-implementation-failure-statistics/) — the largest single enterprise software category remains a SaaS default regardless of agentic coding capability.
- [In 2025, 76% of enterprise AI use cases were purchased rather than built internally, up from 53% in 2024](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/) — the revealed preference of enterprise AI buyers is still strongly toward buy, not build, even as agentic tools mature.
- [Javier Pérez (Edelweiss Capital) assigns the "AI Enhances SaaS" scenario — the analog to SaaS dominance — a 30–40% probability, making it the single most probable outcome in his five-scenario framework](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas) — the modal probability among published analyst scenarios still favors incumbent SaaS.
- [Bain identifies six structural barriers to SaaS displacement — external observability, industry standardization, proprietary data depth, switching/network friction, regulatory/certification barriers, and agent protocol maturity — that keep ERP and security SaaS in "Core Strongholds"](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/) regardless of build cost compression.
- [The METR RCT (16 experienced open-source developers, 246 tasks) found that AI tools caused developers to take 19% longer on their own repositories, while developers believed they were 20% faster](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) — controlled evidence flatly contradicts the productivity claims underpinning the bull build case.

### Base Case (SaaS stays majority but declines, 45–60% market share)

- [35% of enterprises have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom-built alternative, and 78% plan to build more in 2026](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026) — the base case is no longer theoretical; it is already manifesting in internal tools and workflow automation categories.
- [SaaS inflation reached 12.2% annually — 4.5x the G7 CPI — and SaaS costs per employee rose from $7,900 in 2023 to $9,100 by end of 2025](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate) — accelerating vendor price extraction makes the build-vs-buy break-even calculation increasingly favorable for custom software over 3–5 year horizons.
- [Salesforce extracted 72% of its 2025 ARR growth from price increases rather than new customers](https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/) — the pricing-as-revenue-extraction dynamic explicitly motivates the build alternative for large Salesforce customers.
- [Deloitte forecasts that by 2030, 35% of point-product SaaS tools will be replaced by AI agents or absorbed within larger agent ecosystems](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html) — a named institutional forecast anchors the base-case market share erosion figure.
- [Under base scenario assumptions (40–60% build cost compression, maintenance declining to 8–12%), break-even compresses to 18–30 months for commodity workflows — down from the classical 33-month mid-market average](https://xenoss.io/blog/total-cost-of-ownership-for-enterprise-ai) — the financial math now supports base-case erosion for targeted application categories.
- [Bain's "Open Doors" quadrant identifies API value-siphoning as the base-case risk: "In three years, any routine, rules-based digital task could move from 'human plus app' to 'AI agent plus API'"](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/) — seat-based pricing erosion is the mechanism, not full replacement.
- [Organizations waste $21M annually on unused SaaS licenses (52.7% unused), a problem worsening 14.2% year-over-year per Zylo analysis of $40B in managed spend](https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/) — structural waste at this scale creates CFO-level pressure to reduce SaaS exposure in discretionary categories.

### Bear Case (SaaS significantly eroded, <45% market share)

- [Forrester declared "SaaS as we know it is dead" in early 2026, and over $1 trillion in software market cap was erased within a week of rapid AI agent announcements in February 2026](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/) — institutional recognition that the current SaaS model faces structural disruption has crossed from fringe to mainstream.
- [Under bull scenario assumptions (80–95% build cost compression, 1–5% annual maintenance), the effective initial build cost falls to $50K–$200K for mid-tier workflow applications — below the annual SaaS subscription cost for equivalent capability at enterprise scale](https://xenoss.io/blog/total-cost-of-ownership-for-enterprise-ai) — at this cost floor, the economic case for buying commodity-function SaaS collapses.
- [OpenAI's o3 reasoning model dropped 80% in cost over two months; Gartner's best-case scenario places agentic AI driving 30% of enterprise application software revenue by 2035](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/) — AI inference cost deflation at this rate directly expands the build economics in the direction of the bull outcome.
- [Morgan Stanley data shows 50% of software loans were rated B- or lower as of early 2026, with 30% maturing by 2028 — $235B in total software debt facing refinancing in a challenged valuation environment](https://www.thoughtlinks.net/insights/agentic-ai-saas-buy-to-build-framework) — credit market stress in the SaaS vendor base could accelerate consolidation and pricing moderation, but also vendor instability.
- [60% of vendors deliberately mask rising prices by bundling AI features; 28% of contracts experienced "shrinkflation"](https://www.vertice.one/l/saas-inflation-index-report) — if enterprise buyers collectively organize procurement resistance, accelerated switching to custom builds becomes the rational response.
- [A representative large-scale SaaS migration costs ~$1.2M and takes ~8 months; EU Data Act 2025 bans providers from charging for data transfer](https://www.binadox.com/blog/saas-data-portability-planning-your-exit-strategy-before-you-need-it/) — regulatory tailwinds are reducing switching costs, which directly lowers the lock-in floor that has historically protected SaaS market share.
- [Javier Pérez (Edelweiss Capital) assigns a combined 35–55% probability to bull-directional outcomes ("AI Outshines SaaS" at 20–30% plus "AI Cannibalizes SaaS" at 15–25%)](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas) — the aggregate bear probability from a named analyst is higher than any single scenario.

## 5. Critical Caveats for Synthesis

**SaaS inflation rate discrepancy — use Vertice 12.2% as primary:**
T01 (SaaStr) cites SaaS price increases of [approximately 11.4%](https://www.saastr.com/the-great-price-surge-of-2025-a-comprehensive-breakdown-of-pricing-increases-and-the-issues-they-have-created-for-all-of-us/). T04 and T06 (Vertice) cite [12.2% as the SaaS Inflation Index annual rate](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate). The discrepancy likely reflects measurement scope: SaaStr measures announced price increases at named vendors; Vertice measures realized inflation across $40B+ in managed enterprise SaaS contracts, a more representative dataset. Use Vertice 12.2% as the primary figure; note SaaStr 11.4% as a floor for announced increases only.

**T03 category-level TCO claims — treat as directional:**
Section 1 (CRM) cites [americanchase.com](https://americanchase.com/salesforce-implementation-costs/) and [sandis.io](https://sandis.io/resources/tco-custom-software-vs-saas-platforms) for comparative 5-year TCO figures. Neither is a primary research publisher. The directional finding (SaaS wins below 200 users; build competitive above 500 users for CRM) is corroborated by the stronger Sweep/Salesforce data in the same section. Use T03 category verdicts as directional signposts, not precise TCO calculations.

**Agentic productivity range — explicitly bound; weight METR higher:**
The corpus contains a 114-percentage-point spread in productivity claims:
- **Floor (controlled):** [METR RCT (July 2025): 19% slower with AI tools on experienced developers' own repositories](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) — randomized, controlled, compensated, real repositories. This is the most methodologically credible data point in the corpus.
- **Ceiling (uncontrolled vendor claims):** [Times of AI: 70–95% cost reduction from "vibe coding"](https://www.timesofai.com/industry-insights/agentic-coding-in-software-development/); [TELUS: 500,000 hours saved](https://resources.anthropic.com/2026-agentic-coding-trends-report) — self-reported, no control group, publication incentive bias.
- **Mid-range (enterprise RCT):** [GitHub/Kalliamvakou: 55.8% faster on a single isolated task](https://github.blog/news-insights/research/research-quantifying-github-copilots-impact-on-developer-productivity-and-happiness/) — controlled but narrow task scope; [Bain: 10–15% organizational productivity boost, 25–30% with end-to-end process transformation](https://www.bain.com/insights/from-pilots-to-payoff-generative-ai-in-software-development-technology-report-2025/).
- **Position paper bound:** Use METR as the lower bound for conservative scenario modeling, Bain's 25–30% organizational figure as the base case, and the 70–95% vendor claims as the bull scenario ceiling — explicitly labeled as uncontrolled.

**T02 Standish CHAOS failure rates — pre-agentic era, use cautiously:**
The Standish Group CHAOS Report (2020) showing 31% project success / 50% challenged / 19% complete failure is a pre-agentic baseline. It is cited widely in T02 and T04, but the 2020 data does not capture the impact of agentic coding tools on project delivery risk. Use as a historical baseline with explicit note that the failure rate distribution may be shifting. Do not use as a direct argument against build viability in 2026+ without acknowledging the era limitation.

**T06 Zylo $40B managed spend / Vertice 12.2% inflation — strongest SaaS-bear economic evidence in corpus:**
These two data sources represent the most rigorous, large-sample economic evidence for SaaS cost acceleration:
- [Zylo 2025 SaaS Management Index](https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/): drawn from 40M+ SaaS licenses and $40B in managed spend — the largest primary dataset in the Wave 9 corpus. License waste ($21M average / $127M large enterprise), shadow IT (40%+ of spend outside IT oversight), and per-employee spend growth (21.9% YoY) all come from this source.
- [Vertice SaaS Inflation Index](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate): drawn from $1B+ in managed SaaS contracts; 12.2% inflation rate is the most-cited figure for SaaS price escalation in institutional analysis (Deloitte, Forrester, CFO Dive all reference it). Both sources carry the caveat that they represent managed spend visible to the respective platforms — actual enterprise portfolios including shadow IT may show different patterns.

## 6. Break-Even Analysis Summary

From T07, the three-scenario break-even model has the following structure and key thresholds:

### Classical Break-Even Formula
`Cumulative SaaS Cost (Year N) > Initial Build Cost + (Annual Maintenance Rate × Build Cost × N)`

The classical pre-agentic break-even is **18–36 months for most implementations** (VRInSofts); 5-year SaaS spending typically exceeds initial custom build cost by [72%](https://www.vrinsofts.com/saas-subscriptions-vs-custom-software/).

### Scenario Break-Even Thresholds

**Bear Scenario** (20–30% build cost compression; maintenance unchanged at 15–20% of build cost/year):
- Effective initial build cost: $350K–$1.4M (down from $500K–$2M Xenoss baseline)
- Break-even: **2.5–4 years** for commodity workflows; 4–6 years for complex enterprise apps
- Tipping conditions: SaaS pricing must exceed 15%+ annual escalation AND user count must grow 20%+ per year for the economics to favor build within a 3-year window
- POC abandonment risk: ~30% (Gartner), which inflates risk-adjusted effective build cost by ~43%
- SaaS market erosion: **5–10%** of point-product market by 2030
- [Bain analog: "Core Strongholds" quadrant](https://www.bain.com/insights/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas-technology-report-2025/); [Pérez: "AI Enhances SaaS" at 30–40% probability](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas)

**Base Scenario** (40–60% build cost compression; maintenance declining to 8–12%/year with AI-assisted tooling):
- Effective initial build cost: $250K–$1M
- Five-year custom TCO compressed to ~40–50% of pre-agentic equivalent
- Break-even: **18–30 months** for commodity workflows; 30–48 months for complex enterprise apps
- Tipping conditions: Agentic coding tools must demonstrably reduce ongoing maintenance burden (not just initial build) AND governance overhead must stabilize below 20% of project cost
- The Retool survey finding (35% replacement already underway) is the empirical marker that the base scenario is current for early movers
- SaaS market erosion: **25–35%** of point-product market by 2030 ([Deloitte: 35% displacement](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html))
- [Bain analog: "Open Doors" quadrant; Pérez: combined ~45–55% for "AI Outshines + Spending Compresses" scenarios](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas)

**Bull Scenario** (80–95% build cost compression; maintenance approaching 1–5%/year via self-healing architectures):
- Effective initial build cost: $50K–$200K — falling below the [Xenoss "Commercial Platform (Buy)" cost range of $50K–$200K](https://xenoss.io/blog/total-cost-of-ownership-for-enterprise-ai), eliminating the initial cost advantage of SaaS for all but smallest applications
- Break-even: **Immediate to 12 months** for commodity workflows; under 24 months for most enterprise apps above compliance/record-keeping functions
- Tipping conditions: Governance problem must be solved (current AI-generated code introduces [15–18% more security vulnerabilities](https://www.timesofai.com/industry-insights/agentic-coding-in-software-development/) and [4.6x longer review cycles without governance](https://www.timesofai.com/industry-insights/agentic-coding-in-software-development/)); subject-matter experts must be able to build and maintain production software without engineering oversight
- SaaS market erosion: **50–65%** of point-product market by 2030 for non-differentiated tools
- [Bain analog: "Battlegrounds" quadrant; Pérez: "AI Cannibalizes SaaS" at 15–25%](https://edelweisscapital.substack.com/p/will-agentic-ai-disrupt-saas); [Gartner bull-case: 30% of enterprise app revenue from agentic AI by 2035](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-08-26-gartner-predicts-40-percent-of-enterprise-apps-will-feature-task-specific-ai-agents-by-2026-up-from-less-than-5-percent-in-2025)

### Sensitivity Analysis Finding — Highest Leverage Variable

**Annual maintenance cost rate is the highest-leverage variable in the break-even model**, with materially higher impact on 5-year TCO than initial build cost — the variable most commonly cited in public discourse. Specifically:

- A 50% reduction in initial build cost from $1M to $500K saves $500K (one-time).
- A 50% reduction in annual maintenance rate from 20% to 10% on a $1M build saves $100K/year — exceeding the initial saving in year 6 and compounding indefinitely thereafter.
- [Belitsoft's finding that a 90% drop in coding labor translates to only a "10% savings on total expenditures"](https://belitsoft.com/agentic-ai-coding) reflects this exact dynamic: initial labor cost is a smaller fraction of 5-year TCO than commonly assumed.
- **Implication for scenario construction:** The bull scenario is not primarily determined by whether agentic coding can cut build costs by 90% (already claimed). It is determined by whether agentic tools can cut *ongoing maintenance costs* by 80%+ — a claim supported by [one RPA-context analysis (Duvo AI)](https://blog.duvo.ai/why-every-rpa-project-breaks-and-how-agentic-ai-fixes-it) but not yet verified in broad enterprise software contexts.

Secondary sensitivity variables (ranked):
1. User count and growth rate — break-even "happens faster with 20+ users, multiple SaaS tools, or annual price increases above 10%; weakens materially below 15–20 users" ([VRInSofts](https://www.vrinsofts.com/saas-subscriptions-vs-custom-software/))
2. Evaluation time horizon — 2-year analysis almost always favors buying; 5-year analysis increasingly favors building ([Robert DeSisto, Gartner: "SaaS loses its TCO advantage beginning in year three"](https://www.infoworld.com/article/2221749/gartner-don-t-assume-saas-is-cheaper.html))
3. SaaS vendor price escalation rate — at the upper bound of current escalation (25%/year), SaaS cost quadruples over five years, making build favorable before year three regardless of scenario
4. Initial build cost — most cited, lowest long-run leverage

## 7. Wave Verdict

On purely financial grounds, Wave 9's TCO and economic data supports the base case as the most probable scenario, with the bear case being a legitimate upper-probability tail, not a fringe outcome. The SaaS model's TCO structure is demonstrably broken in ways that are not cyclical: 52.7% of licenses go unused, SaaS inflation runs at 4.5x general CPI, and Salesforce — the category leader — derived 72% of its 2025 revenue growth from price increases rather than new customers. These are not optimization failures; they are the structural outputs of a captive-customer pricing model, and enterprise CFOs now have the quantitative evidence to act on them. The build alternative has become financially credible in a specific and widening category band — internal tools, workflow automation, and mid-market CRM — where the Retool data shows 35% replacement is not a future intention but a present reality. However, the financial case for building collapses precisely where enterprise software spend is most concentrated: ERP (the largest single SaaS category at $44B, 20% of enterprise SaaS), compliance, and security — categories where the regulatory update obligation and failure rate (55–75% for ERP projects) make custom build economically irrational under any current scenario. The critical unknown that determines whether we are in the base case (25–35% erosion) or bear case (50–65% erosion) is not whether agentic coding can cut initial build costs — it demonstrably can, at meaningful scale, for targeted applications. The unknown is whether agentic tools can reduce *ongoing maintenance costs* from the classical 15–20% annual rate toward 1–5%. That single variable, currently supported only by RPA-context evidence and vendor claims, has more leverage on the 5-year SaaS market share outcome than any other factor in the corpus.
