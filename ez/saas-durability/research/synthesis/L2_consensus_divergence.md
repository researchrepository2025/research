## 1. High-Confidence Consensus (≥7 of 9 Waves Agree)

These findings are the bedrock facts for the position paper. They appear independently across perspectives from researchers, operators, investors, consultants, and economists — the combination of independent sourcing across different methodologies is what makes them high-confidence.

---

### C1. Seat-Based SaaS Pricing Is Structurally Broken and Will Not Survive as the Dominant Model

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 (all nine)

Every wave reaches this conclusion through independent pathways. Wave 1 (Foundation) documents seat-based pricing falling from [21% to 15% of SaaS market share in a single year](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/) and IDC projecting 70% of vendors will refactor by 2028. Wave 2 (VC) shows a16z partners Martin Casado and Alex Rampell declaring per-seat models obsolete. Wave 3 (Enterprise CTO/CIO) documents AI enabling productivity gains that reduce seat counts without reducing workflow coverage. Wave 4 (Vendor) shows the largest SaaS incumbents — Salesforce, ServiceNow, Workday — all actively abandoning per-seat pricing for usage- and outcome-based models. Wave 5 (Consulting) documents BCG finding [40% of IT buyers cite seat reduction as their primary cost lever](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/rethinking-b2b-software-pricing-in-the-era-of-ai). Wave 6 (Developer) shows [multiple SaaS companies reporting in Q4 2025 that customers are reducing seats](https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/saaspocalypse-ai-agents-software-industry-analysis) as AI-enhanced workers accomplish more with fewer licenses. Wave 7 (Equity) documents the pricing model collapse driving the equity selloff. Wave 9 (TCO) shows [Salesforce extracting 72% of its 2025 ARR growth from price increases, not new customers](https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/).

**Key distinction for position paper:** This consensus is specifically about the *pricing model*, not the *SaaS procurement modality*. All nine waves treat pricing disruption and category extinction as separate claims. The position paper must honor this distinction.

---

### C2. SaaS in Absolute Dollar Terms Is Growing, Not Contracting

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 (8 of 9; Wave 8's agentic coding focus does not foreground spend data)

Forrester projects global SaaS spending rising from [$318 billion in 2025 to $576 billion by 2029](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/). Gartner forecasts [software spending growing 14.7% in 2026 to $1.4 trillion](https://www.saastr.com/gartner-business-software-spend-will-grow-a-stunning-14-7-in-2026-to-1-4-trillion-up-from-11-5-in-2025-are-you-grabbing-it/). BCG documents SaaS spending growing from [13% to 21% of enterprise IT budgets over five years](https://www.ciodive.com/news/saas-software-services-spend-surges-bcg/801732/). Zylo finds average enterprise SaaS spend growing to [$55.7M in 2026, up 8% year-over-year](https://zylo.com/reports/2026-saas-management-index/). This consensus is confirmed by financial data (Wave 7), enterprise buyer data (Wave 3), and consulting research (Wave 5) independently.

**Implication:** Any position paper claim that SaaS is "losing" must be framed as a *share* story, not an *absolute dollar* story. The category is expanding even if its share of software procurement is declining.

### C3. Regulated, High-Complexity, Data-Gravity SaaS (ERP, Healthcare, Financial Systems) Is Structurally Durable in the 2–5 Year Window

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 (8 of 9; Wave 8 adds nuance on benchmarks but concurs on regulated segments)

Wave 1 documents that every prior disruption wave (cloud, open source, low-code) produced absorption rather than displacement in segments with deep data integration. Wave 2 (VC) shows Thoma Bravo's [$34.4 billion capital raise](https://capwolf.com/orlando-bravo-why-software-stocks-are-oversold-now/) explicitly betting on domain-expertise SaaS durability. Wave 3 (Enterprise) documents Capital One building proprietary agentic stacks *alongside* maintained vendor relationships. Wave 4 (Vendor) cites Epic's [16.3 billion patient encounters in Cosmos](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/epic-unveils-ai-agents-showcases-new-foundational-models), Veeva's ~80% life sciences CRM market share, and compliance certifications (HIPAA, FDA 21 CFR Part 11, FedRAMP) that take 2–5 years to establish. Wave 5 (Consulting) documents Forrester's Charles Betz citing [20,000 legal jurisdictions](https://www.theregister.com/2026/02/04/ai_replace_saas/) and BCG showing ERP and CRM regaining momentum as AI adoption foundations. Wave 7 (Equity) shows Thoma Bravo completing a [$12.3 billion Dayforce take-private](https://www.corpdev.org/2025/06/05/thoma-bravo-secures-34-4-billion-buyout-fund-haul-decoding-the-software-sectors-new-power-play/) as confidence in regulated SaaS. Wave 9 (TCO) finds ERP implementation failure rates of 55–75%, making custom-build "economically irrational for most enterprises" in that category regardless of agentic tool capability.

**The one nuance across waves:** All agree on the *2–5 year* window. Several waves (1, 5, 8) note that beyond 5 years, agentic AI capability improvements could reach regulated categories too — but the position paper's scope is 2–5 years, where the consensus is robust.

### C4. Point-Solution, Horizontal, Low-Switching-Cost SaaS Is Facing Genuine Displacement Pressure Already

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 (8 of 9)

This is the counterpart to C3. Where C3 identifies durable categories, C4 identifies vulnerable ones. Wave 1 (Foundation) cites Forrester declaring horizontal point-solution vendors with low switching costs face "structural challenges." Wave 2 (VC) documents Bain's "seat compression" as the mechanism: ["Rather than 500 seats, customers might purchase 450 and deploy agents for marginal usage"](https://www.bain.com/insights/why-saas-stocks-have-dropped-and-what-it-signals-for-softwares-next-chapter/). Wave 3 (Enterprise) shows Retool's survey documenting [35% replacement in workflow automations (35%), internal admin tools (33%), BI tools (29%), CRMs (25%)](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026). Wave 4 (Vendor) cites Bain explicitly naming HubSpot list-building and Monday.com task boards as already-feasible substitution targets. Wave 5 (Consulting) documents standalone analytics declining 18 percentage points in IT budgets as it gets subsumed into broader AI initiatives. Wave 6 (Developer) shows the developer community's consensus taxonomy: CRUD-adjacent, workflow, and narrow internal tools face near-term displacement. Wave 9 (TCO) shows 18–30 month break-even compressing in commodity workflows under base scenario build cost assumptions.

**Gartner's quantification** (cited by Waves 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9): [By 2030, 35% of point-product SaaS tools will be replaced by AI agents or absorbed within larger agent ecosystems](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html). This is the most-cited single institutional forecast across the corpus.

### C5. Enterprise Execution Reality Significantly Lags Narrative: Fewer Than 25% of Enterprises Have Production-Grade Agentic Deployments

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 (8 of 9)

Wave 1 cites Deloitte's State of AI finding [only 21% of organizations have mature governance models for autonomous agents](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/state-of-ai-report-2026.html). Wave 2 (VC) documents BofA finding [only 6% of surveyed firms had AI agents in production as of October 2025](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/saas-stock-selloff-overdone-says-bofa-4268645). Wave 3 (Enterprise) shows S&P Global finding [42% of AI initiatives abandoned before production, up from 17% in 2024](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/ai-experiences-rapid-adoption-but-with-mixed-outcomes-highlights-from-vote-ai-machine-learning). Wave 5 (Consulting) cites BCG's finding that [only 5% of companies are achieving AI value at scale; 60% generating no material value](https://media-publications.bcg.com/The-Widening-AI-Value-Gap-Sept-2025.pdf). Wave 7 (Equity) finds [only 4 of 150 CIOs raised hands on agentic AI deployment in enterprise settings](https://fintool.com/app/research/analyst/sanjit-singh) in Morgan Stanley's survey. Wave 8 (Agentic Coding) documents the 87.5% digital transformation failure rate and MIT NANDA's finding that [95% of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to achieve rapid revenue acceleration](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/). Wave 9 (TCO) confirms Gartner's 40%+ agentic project cancellation forecast as the mechanism.

**The implication for the position paper:** The disruption narrative is approximately 3–5 years ahead of the deployment reality. This gap is the primary near-term protector of SaaS market share.

### C6. The February 2026 "SaaSpocalypse" ($1 Trillion+ Equity Selloff) Was a Sentiment Event, Not a Revenue Event

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 (8 of 9)

All waves documenting the event characterize it as driven by fear of future disruption, not evidence of current disruption. Wave 1 cites J.P. Morgan explicitly calling the sell-off logic ["broken"](https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/02/17/jp-morgan-research-says-broken-logic-is-driving-th/) while software revenue data showed continued growth. Wave 2 (VC) flags that the Citrini Research report driving "Black Tuesday" was authored by a "little known firm" whose author admitted the market reaction was "larger than we expected." Wave 7 (Equity) confirms: [no actual customer cancellation data, churn rates, or NRR changes attributable directly to AI agent displacement were found in primary sources as of March 2026](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas). Enterprise SaaS gross retention holds at ~90%. Wave 9 documents software sector ROIC of 50.17% vs. WACC of 9.34% — the underlying economics remain healthy despite the equity selloff.

**Caveat:** Multiple waves note that sentiment events can become self-fulfilling through reduced investment in SaaS tooling and accelerated build decisions. The equity signal is not meaningless — it is simply not yet confirmed by operating metrics.

### C7. The Build-vs-Buy Shift Is Already Occurring at the Margins, Not at the Core

**Waves agreeing:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 (8 of 9)

This is the most precisely calibrated cross-wave consensus in the corpus. The shift is documented, real, and directional — but its locus is specific. Wave 3 (Enterprise) and Wave 4 (Vendor) both cite Retool's 35% replacement figure, while simultaneously noting that David Hsu (Retool's own CEO) scoped it precisely: ["Nobody's ripping out Salesforce wholesale. What they're doing is replacing the specific piece of a tool that never quite fit."](https://www.newsweek.com/nw-ai/enterprises-are-replacing-saas-faster-than-you-think-11521483) Wave 6 (Developer) independently taxonomizes the vulnerable categories (workflow automations, internal admin tools, BI tools) as CRUD-adjacent and narrowly scoped. Wave 9 (TCO) confirms these are exactly the categories where the 18–30 month break-even has already been crossed in base-case build economics.

**What makes this different from C4:** C4 is about *which categories* face pressure. C7 is about the *scope and pace* of the shift — it is already occurring but is geographically bounded to the margins, not spreading to core systems.

## 3. Contested Findings (Waves Genuinely Disagree)

---

### D1. How Fast Can AI-Assisted Coding Actually Accelerate Custom Builds at Enterprise Scale?

**Claim disputed:** Agentic coding tools cut enterprise custom software development costs by 50–95%.

**Waves supporting the high-productivity claim:**
- Wave 1 cites Anthropic's Agentic Coding Trends Report documenting [30–79% faster development cycles, Rakuten completing 12.5M-line codebase modifications in 7 hours at 99.9% accuracy](https://resources.anthropic.com/2026-agentic-coding-trends-report).
- Wave 4 cites vibe coding tools cutting development costs by [70–95%](https://www.ibbaka.com/ibbaka-market-blog/b2b-saas-and-agentic-ai-pricing-predictions-for-2026).
- Wave 6 documents Replit generating [2 million apps in six months without users writing a single line of code](https://replit.com/news/funding-announcement).

**Waves contradicting the high-productivity claim:**
- Wave 8 (the most forensic source on this question) presents the METR RCT finding that [experienced developers using AI tools took 19% longer on complex mature codebases](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) — a negative productivity effect.
- Wave 8 further documents [on SWE-bench Pro's commercial codebase subset, the best model achieves only 9.1% Pass@1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.16941), compared to 77%+ on standard SWE-bench Verified.
- Wave 6 cites Bain finding [98% more merged PRs produced no measurable company-level delivery improvement](https://www.bain.com/insights/from-pilots-to-payoff-generative-ai-in-software-development-technology-report-2025/).

**Nature of disagreement:** This is primarily a *methodological* and *task-scope* disagreement. The high-productivity claims come from vendor-reported outcomes on *greenfield*, *bounded*, *verifiable* tasks (scaffolding, migration, test generation) conducted by early adopters with strong tooling. The low-productivity findings come from *controlled experiments* on *mature*, *complex*, *multi-file* codebases using *experienced* developers — the most relevant profile for enterprise custom-build as SaaS replacement.

**How to handle in position paper:** Report both. Frame the METR RCT as the most methodologically credible available evidence for enterprise-scale custom builds. Frame the vendor claims as upper bounds for specifically bounded, greenfield tasks. The position paper should use a tiered productivity estimate: 70–95% cost reduction for bounded internal tools; 10–25% net organizational gain for complex enterprise software; negative net effect on highly mature codebases until tooling improves.

### D2. Is SaaS Spending Growth Evidence of Resilience or Inflation-Driven Illusion?

**Claim disputed:** The fact that SaaS spending is growing to $576 billion by 2029 proves SaaS is durable.

**Waves using this as bull-case evidence:**
- Waves 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7 all cite the Forrester $576B projection and/or Gartner's 14.7% software spending growth as evidence of SaaS category health.

**Waves challenging the interpretation:**
- Wave 9 (TCO) documents [SaaS inflation at 12.2% annually — 4.5x general CPI](https://www.vertice.one/insights/saas-inflation-rate), and [Salesforce extracting 72% of 2025 ARR growth from price increases](https://www.saastr.com/salesforce-microsoft-google-and-atlassian-all-raise-prices-again-in-2025-hooray/) — implying the spending growth reflects pricing power over captive customers, not expanding procurement adoption.
- Wave 7 (Equity) cites Gartner's John-David Lovelock: ["GenAI features are now ubiquitous across software already owned and operated by enterprises and these features cost more money. The cost of software is going up"](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-22-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-9-point-8-percent-in-2026-exceeding-6-trillion-dollars-for-the-first-time) — framing growth as inflation, not adoption expansion.
- Wave 9 independently notes that Zylo finds [52.7% of purchased SaaS licenses go unused](https://zylo.com/news/2025-saas-management-index/), meaning spending growth may be partly waste expansion rather than value delivery expansion.

**Nature of disagreement:** *Definitional*. The bull case interprets spending growth as evidence of demand health. The skeptical case interprets it as captive-customer price extraction combined with AI feature bundling that artificially inflates the headline number. Both can be simultaneously true — the SaaS market can be both growing in nominal terms and facing structural share erosion.

**How to handle in position paper:** Separate absolute dollar figures from market share trajectory. Acknowledge that spending growth is real but distinguish between growth-from-adoption and growth-from-price-extraction. The two strongest data points in tension: Gartner's 14.7% software growth and Vertice's 12.2% SaaS inflation rate. If inflation accounts for ~12% of the ~15% growth, the real-terms volume expansion is approximately 3% — not the bullish story the headline implies.

### D3. Is the February 2026 Equity Selloff Signal or Noise for Future SaaS Revenue?

**Claim disputed:** The ~$1 trillion equity selloff signals a genuine structural risk to SaaS revenue.

**Waves treating it as signal:**
- Wave 7 (Equity): Goldman Sachs strategist Ben Snider drew the [newspaper-industry analogy](https://www.thestreet.com/investing/stocks/goldman-sachs-signals-grim-shift-as-software-stocks-bounce), and Jefferies cut Workday to $150 and DocuSign to $45. Tomasz Tunguz documented that "vertical software down 43% YTD" reflects market pricing of structural disruption, not cyclical correction.
- Wave 5 (Consulting): [$1 trillion in software market cap erased in the first week of February 2026](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/) is cited as the most dramatic single-event market signal in the dataset.

**Waves treating it as noise:**
- Waves 1, 2, 6, 7 (partially): JPMorgan's ["broken logic"](https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/02/17/jp-morgan-research-says-broken-logic-is-driving-th/) characterization, J.P. Morgan calling the selloff an "overshoot," and Wave 2 explicitly flagging Citrini Research as a "little known firm" whose report triggered the event.
- Wave 7 (its own caveat): "No actual customer cancellation data, churn rates, or NRR changes attributable directly to AI agent displacement were found in primary sources as of March 2026."

**Nature of disagreement:** *Temporal* — the bear camp says equity markets lead fundamentals by 12–24 months and the revenue data will follow; the bull camp says the selloff is disconnected from fundamentals and will reverse when actual churn data fails to materialize. This is genuinely unresolvable with March 2026 data. Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan hold opposite positions from equal analytical vantage points.

**How to handle in position paper:** Treat the selloff as a *sentiment indicator* with some leading-indicator properties — not as direct evidence of revenue disruption. Acknowledge the Goldman newspaper analogy as the most sophisticated bear-case framing while noting JPMorgan's "broken logic" rebuttal and the absence of confirming hard financial data as of the position paper's writing date.

### D4. Are Agentic AI Projects Primarily a Near-Term Distraction or a Durable Competitive Lever?

**Claim disputed:** The 40%+ agentic AI project cancellation rate (Gartner) constrains the build-vs-buy threat.

**Waves using cancellation rate as bull-case evidence:**
- Waves 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 all cite the Gartner forecast that [over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027) as moderating the bear case timeline.

**Waves challenging the interpretation:**
- Wave 8 (Agentic Coding) notes that the same Gartner source predicting 40% cancellations also predicts 40% of enterprise apps will feature AI agents by 2026 — implying these are not the same projects. The "canceled" projects are likely experimental, not mature builds.
- Wave 5 (Consulting) documents McKinsey observing enterprises "retrenching — rehiring people where agents have failed" as a near-term pattern, but simultaneously projecting long-term adoption. The retrenchment is not exit from the category; it is maturation.
- Wave 3 (Enterprise CTO) notes S&P Global's finding that pilot abandonment surged from 17% to 42% in a single year — arguably the *acceleration* of the learning curve, not evidence of structural failure.

**Nature of disagreement:** *Interpretive*. Everyone agrees on the 40% figure. The disagreement is whether this indicates the build alternative will fail broadly, or whether it indicates the early 60% that survive are building the proof of concept for the bear case scenario. High failure rates characterized cloud adoption in 2010–2012 too — cloud still became dominant.

**How to handle in position paper:** Use the 40% cancellation figure as evidence that the bear case *timeline* is extended, not that it is *invalidated*. Frame it as: "The first wave of agentic projects will fail at high rates; the survivors will define the baseline for the second wave, which arrives by 2028–2029."

### D5. Does Enterprise Buyer Preference Show Continuation of Buy-Over-Build, or Increasing Build Intent?

**Claim disputed:** The direction of the buy-vs.-build ratio in enterprise AI.

**Waves supporting the "buy is increasing" narrative:**
- Waves 1, 2, 4, 8 all cite Menlo Ventures' finding that [76% of enterprise AI use cases are now purchased rather than built — a reversal from 47%/53% in 2024](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/). Wave 2 adds that 65% of Global 2000 CIOs prefer incumbent solutions when available. Wave 4 documents the a16z CIO survey showing a "marked shift towards buying third-party applications over the last twelve months."

**Waves supporting the "build intent is rising" narrative:**
- Waves 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 9 all cite Retool's finding that [35% of enterprises have already replaced at least one SaaS tool and 78% intend to build more in 2026](https://retool.com/blog/ai-build-vs-buy-report-2026). Wave 3 documents shadow IT builders being senior managers (64%), meaning build intent is executive-sponsored, not grassroots.

**Nature of disagreement:** *Sample bias* and *definitional*. The Menlo Ventures survey (n=~500 U.S. enterprise AI decision-makers) captures the *AI procurement* decision — and indeed enterprises are buying AI models, AI applications, and AI platforms rather than building their own LLMs. The Retool survey (n=817, biased toward builders) captures the *individual software category* replacement decision — where specific workflow tools are being displaced. These are measuring different things. You can simultaneously be buying 76% of your AI stack *and* building custom tools to replace some SaaS subscriptions, without contradiction.

**How to handle in position paper:** Use both figures with the disambiguation: enterprises are buying AI infrastructure (the Menlo finding) while building custom applications on top of it that replace some SaaS subscriptions (the Retool finding). The two are complementary, not contradictory.

### D6. Does the "AI Cannibalizes SaaS" Scenario Apply to Systems of Record?

**Claim disputed:** Satya Nadella's thesis that SaaS applications "will all collapse" because "they are essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic" that AI agents will manage.

**Waves supporting Nadella's collapse thesis:**
- Waves 2, 3, 4, 6 all cite Nadella's statement as the highest-credibility bear-case declaration in the corpus.
- Wave 4 adds Bill McDermott (ServiceNow CEO) saying "functional SaaS and feature SaaS will be automated."
- Wave 3 cites Bill Vass (Booz Allen Hamilton CTO) and Tatyana Mamut (former Salesforce VP) predicting structural disintermediation.

**Waves contradicting the collapse thesis for systems of record:**
- Wave 2 (VC) cites Thoma Bravo's Orlando Bravo: ["Software is about domain knowledge, not code"](https://www.semafor.com/article/01/27/2026/wait-for-it-to-pop-orlando-bravo-on-the-ai-bubble-and-vibe-coding-revolution) and Workday's Aneel Bhusri: ["No amount of vibe coding will ever produce an HR or ERP system that meets modern business needs."](https://cloudwars.com/cloud-wars-minute/workday-ceo-bhusri-brilliant-take-on-apps-ai-and-limits-of-vibe-coding/)
- Wave 4 (Vendor) shows ServiceNow with 98% renewal rate and Epic with 16.3 billion patient encounters in its proprietary dataset — neither behaving like a "CRUD database."
- Wave 9 (TCO) shows ERP implementation failure rates of 55–75%, making the build alternative financially irrational regardless of Nadella's characterization.

**Nature of disagreement:** *Stakeholder-specific* and *definitional*. Nadella is speaking about the long-term architectural future; Bravo and Bhusri are speaking about the near-term competitive reality. Both can be correct across different time horizons. The CRUD characterization is architecturally accurate — what it ignores is that the value of these systems is not the code but the data, the integrations, the 30 years of domain logic baked in, the audit trails, and the regulatory certifications. Those are not CRUD problems; they are organizational problems that agentic coding cannot solve.

**How to handle in position paper:** Acknowledge Nadella's thesis as a credible long-term architectural claim while noting it underspecifies the non-code components of enterprise software value. Use Bhusri's ERP rebuttal and the 55–75% ERP failure rate as near-term counterarguments. The position paper can accept both: the *code* of business applications may commoditize within a decade; the *knowledge*, *data*, and *compliance infrastructure* embedded in systems of record will not commoditize within 5 years.

## 5. Structural Gaps

The following questions are materially unanswered by the 9-wave corpus. Each would significantly shift scenario probabilities if answered.

---

### Gap 1: What Is the Actual Agentic Maintenance Cost Rate?

**Why it matters (Wave 9 finding):** Wave 9's break-even analysis identifies *annual maintenance cost rate* as the single highest-leverage variable in the build-vs-buy economic model — more impactful on 5-year TCO than initial build cost. Classical software maintenance costs 15–20% of initial build cost annually. If agentic tools reduce this to 5%, the break-even calculation shifts dramatically in favor of building. If the maintenance cost stays at 15–20% (because AI-generated code creates technical debt faster than agentic maintenance can clear it), the economics remain unfavorable for building. The corpus contains exactly one source addressing this directly — a Duvo AI analysis in the RPA context — and it is vendor-produced and methodologically opaque. The position paper cannot resolve which scenario is correct; it can only identify this as the most consequential unknown.

**What would change:** If independent evidence showed that AI-assisted maintenance reduces the effective annual rate to 5–8%, the bear case probability increases substantially and the 2–5 year timeline compresses. If evidence showed AI-generated code compounds technical debt (consistent with the Wave 8 findings on architectural complexity spikes), the bull case strengthens.

---

### Gap 2: What Happens to SaaS NRR in Enterprise Accounts as Seat Compression Materializes?

**Why it matters:** Multiple waves note that seat compression is theoretically occurring — enterprises reducing seats as AI-enhanced workers accomplish more with fewer licenses. But as of March 2026, [no actual NRR changes attributable directly to AI agent displacement have been found in primary sources](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/2026/03/02/is-ai-killing-saas). The Q3 and Q4 2026 earnings cycles will be the first real-world test. If NRR falls below 100% for major SaaS categories, the bear case gains hard financial evidence. If NRR holds at 108%+, the bull case is confirmed at the model level.

**What would change:** A verified, attributable 5%+ NRR decline in seat-based SaaS enterprise accounts across two consecutive quarters would be the single data point most capable of shifting consensus from base case to bear case.

---

### Gap 3: Does the METR Task-Horizon Doubling Continue?

**Why it matters:** The METR task-completion time horizon has been doubling every 88.6–196.5 days. If this continues, agents capable of autonomous week-long software projects arrive between 2027 and 2029. If the trajectory plateaus (as AI scaling often does when it approaches fundamental limits), the bear case recedes to post-2030. The corpus does not contain forward-looking empirical evidence on this — it is fundamentally a forecasting question.

**What would change:** A verified plateau in METR task-horizon improvement over two consecutive measurement periods would be the strongest available evidence for a longer SaaS durability window. Continued doubling would be the strongest signal for accelerating the bear case timeline.

---

### Gap 4: What Fraction of the 35% SaaS Replacement Cases Involved Core Business Logic vs. Peripheral Tools?

**Why it matters:** The Retool survey's 35% replacement figure is the most-cited behavioral data point in the corpus. But it does not distinguish between: (a) replacing a lightweight internal dashboard SaaS with a custom build — a trivial substitution with low organizational risk; and (b) replacing a core workflow SaaS (CRM, project management) with a custom build — a strategically significant substitution. The position paper's argument depends heavily on which type of replacement is driving the 35%. If case (b) is significant, the base case erodes toward the bear. If case (a) dominates, the bull case is largely intact.

**What would change:** A segmented breakdown of the 35% by application criticality tier would change how the build-vs-buy shift should be characterized in the position paper. A neutral enterprise procurement survey (not from a build-platform vendor) covering the same question would also help address the sampling bias.

---

### Gap 5: How Do Mid-Market Enterprises (500–10,000 Employees) Behave vs. Fortune 500?

**Why it matters:** The two main enterprise survey instruments in the corpus cover different populations. The a16z CIO survey (n=100) and Deloitte CATO survey skew toward Fortune 500. The Retool survey (n=817) skews toward technically sophisticated mid-market builders. The BCG Widening AI Value Gap (n=1,250+) spans both but does not segment by company size. The mid-market is where the "punch above your weight" build thesis is most compelling — these companies are large enough to have engineering capacity but small enough to feel the SaaS inflation pain acutely. The corpus almost completely lacks primary data from this segment at the decision-maker level.

**What would change:** If mid-market enterprises (the largest segment by count of companies) are shifting to build at the 35% rate the Retool survey implies, the aggregate market share erosion could exceed the base case projection. If they are buying AI SaaS at the 76% rate the Menlo survey implies, the base case holds.

---

### Gap 6: Does AI-Native SaaS Count as SaaS or as the Replacement of SaaS?

**Why it matters:** Wave 1 identifies this definitional ambiguity as "Caveat 8" and it is echoed by Waves 2, 4, and 7. [Zylo documents AI-native application spend growing 393% YoY](https://zylo.com/reports/2026-saas-management-index/) — and ChatGPT is now the most expensed application in enterprise portfolios. Is enterprise adoption of ChatGPT Enterprise evidence of SaaS dominance (enterprises are buying AI SaaS), or evidence of SaaS displacement (enterprises are replacing legacy SaaS with AI-native SaaS)? The answer determines whether the current trend confirms or confutes the bull case. Wave 5 explicitly notes that Deloitte's 60% AI-agent TAM by 2030 is compatible with SaaS dominance if AI-powered SaaS is counted as SaaS.

**What would change:** A clear taxonomy distinguishing "AI-native SaaS" from "custom AI builds" from "AI-enhanced legacy SaaS" would sharpen all scenario probability estimates. Without this, market share calculations are not comparable across sources.

## 6. The Central Tension

The 9-wave corpus circles one irreducible question that the position paper must address directly: **Is the enterprise software market undergoing a business model disruption (which SaaS will absorb and survive) or an architectural disruption (which will eventually hollow out SaaS's reason to exist)?**

The evidence on the business model disruption side is robust and multi-wave corroborated. Per-seat pricing is dying — [Forrester documents seat-based pricing falling from 21% to 15% market share in a single year](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/saas-as-we-know-it-is-dead-how-to-survive-the-saas-pocalypse/), IDC projects 70% of vendors will refactor by 2028, and the major incumbents (Salesforce at 200% ARR growth on Agentforce, ServiceNow at 98% renewals, HubSpot's successful pivot to consumption models) are demonstrating that they can absorb this disruption by shifting to usage- and outcome-based pricing while deepening, not losing, customer relationships. Historical precedents (cloud vs. on-premise, open source vs. commercial) all favor this absorption thesis. If this is the correct frame, SaaS as a *procurement modality* — buying rather than building enterprise software — survives with market share in the 50–60% range, pricing models simply evolve, and the position paper's answer is "yes, SaaS remains dominant."

The evidence on the architectural disruption side is directional, less corroborated, but structurally serious. Satya Nadella's argument that business applications ["are essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic"](https://www.windowscentral.com/microsoft/hey-why-do-i-need-excel-microsoft-ceo-satya-nadella-foresees-a-disruptive-agentic-ai-era-that-could-aggressively-collapse-saas-apps) that AI agents will manage without a SaaS intermediary — combined with McKinsey's "post-SaaS architecture" where ["a human employee primarily interacts with one agent interface... while back-end agents and APIs act directly on data repositories"](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/upgrading-software-business-models-to-thrive-in-the-ai-era) — describes a world where the SaaS application layer is genuinely disintermediated. The METR task-horizon doubling rate implies this capability could arrive within the 5-year window. Goldman Sachs' newspaper industry analogy suggests the revenue data will lag the structural shift by years. If this is the correct frame, the question of which pricing model wins becomes secondary to the question of whether *any* independent software application survives as an addressable market. The position paper's answer would be "no, SaaS will not remain dominant."

The most honest synthesis of the corpus's evidence is that both framings are *simultaneously operative* in different segments of the SaaS market. The business model disruption frame is correct for Tier 1 platform SaaS (Salesforce, ServiceNow, SAP, Microsoft) and regulated vertical SaaS (Epic, Veeva, Guidewire) — these companies will survive by transforming. The architectural disruption frame is increasingly correct for the point-solution long tail — the [35% of point-product SaaS tools Gartner and Deloitte project to be replaced by 2030](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html). The central tension is therefore not a binary but a boundary-drawing problem: **where exactly does the line run between "SaaS categories that will absorb the disruption" and "SaaS categories that will be architecturally disintermediated" — and how far does that line move within the 2–5 year window the position paper addresses?** All evidence in the corpus converges on the answer that, within 2–5 years, the line runs at approximately the boundary between platform SaaS / regulated vertical SaaS (durable) and horizontal point-solution SaaS / workflow tool SaaS (structurally exposed) — and that today's [35% replacement of the latter is the early signal of a displacement curve that will reach 35% of the point-product market by 2030](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/saas-ai-agents.html), while leaving the former largely intact.
