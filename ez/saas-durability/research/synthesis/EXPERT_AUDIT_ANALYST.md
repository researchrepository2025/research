# Expert Audit: Analyst & Consulting Firm Positions
## SaaS Durability Research — Buy-vs-Build Camp Assignment Review
**Audit Date:** 2026-03-06
**Auditor scope:** Gartner, Forrester, IDC, McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte analysts and named partners only
**Research question:** Will SaaS remain the majority enterprise procurement method (vs. custom build) for AI-driven applications in 2–5 years?

---

## Audit Criterion (Applied Throughout)

A direct, explicit statement about whether enterprises will predominantly *buy SaaS vs. build custom software* for AI-driven applications qualifies for camp assignment. Market sizing forecasts, spending growth projections, AI adoption rates, and technology disruption risk assessments do NOT qualify unless the author explicitly connects them to the buy-vs-build procurement modality. Where an author addressed the buy-vs-build question but with conditions or caveats, the assignment is NUANCED-CONDITIONAL. Where the author's primary statements are about adjacent metrics, the assignment is UNCLEAR.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

### 1. Over-attribution is systematic, not exceptional
The majority of analyst and consulting firm entries in the L2 registry were assigned to BULL or BASE based on statements about SaaS market growth, AI adoption rates, vendor survival, or pricing model evolution. None of these are equivalent to a statement about enterprise procurement modality (buy vs. build). Of 23 analysts and partners audited, only 8 made statements that directly or partially address the buy-vs-build question. The remaining 15 are currently mis-assigned based on adjacent evidence.

### 2. The "spending forecast = buy endorsement" error is the most common mistake
Multiple analysts (Lovelock, Nag, Kropp, Della Rosa) were assigned BASE or BULL based on spending growth projections. A forecast that SaaS spending will grow to $576B does not mean enterprises will predominantly *choose* SaaS over custom builds — it is compatible with both: a growing overall IT market could include growing SaaS spend even while custom build captures an increasing share of new AI-driven application development. These are not the same metric.

### 3. Conflict of interest is universal across all firms audited
Every firm audited (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, McKinsey, Bain, BCG, Deloitte) has bilateral conflict of interest: they earn from SaaS vendor advisory services AND from custom implementation/build engagements. The structural incentive across all of these firms is to produce "hybrid" or "neither extreme" conclusions that preserve both revenue streams. This systematic conflict does not invalidate their research, but it means independent corroboration is required before treating any single analyst's position as conclusive evidence on the buy-vs-build question. None of the firms' positions should be cited as independent data points relative to each other — they share the same structural incentive.

### 4. Denis Torii (Gartner) and Jeremy Schneider (McKinsey) are the highest-quality direct buy-vs-build sources
Torii's "Buy, Build, Blend" and McKinsey's published Make-or-Buy Matrix are the only analyst/consulting outputs in the corpus that explicitly frame enterprise procurement as a framework choice. Both are NUANCED-CONDITIONAL, not BASE. Both refuse to predict SaaS majority dominance.

### 5. The Forrester "SaaS-pocalypse" internal contradiction requires explicit acknowledgment
Kate Leggett's February 2026 report simultaneously contains the headline "SaaS As We Know It Is Dead" (bear-directional) and a $576B spending forecast (bull-directional in the same document). Using either statement in isolation to assign a camp misrepresents Forrester's actual position. Leggett's real position is NUANCED-CONDITIONAL: survival depends on vendor category (vertical/specialized survives; horizontal point-solutions do not).

### 6. No analyst or consulting partner in this corpus predicted custom build will become the *majority* enterprise procurement method for AI-driven applications
The bear camp position (custom build wins majority share) has no direct analyst or consulting firm support in the corpus. The bear case is supported by VC narratives and AI-native startup founders — not by named analysts at research or consulting firms. This is relevant to the overall research synthesis: the institutional analyst consensus, even with all its conflicts, does not include a single named expert predicting build-majority outcomes.
