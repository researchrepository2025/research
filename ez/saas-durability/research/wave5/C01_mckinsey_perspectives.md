# C01 — McKinsey Perspectives: Enterprise SaaS Durability & AI Impact

**Research stream:** Enterprise SaaS Durability in the Agentic Coding Era
**Scope:** McKinsey & Company published positions only (2025–2026)
**Audience:** C-suite
**Last updated:** 2026-03-06
**Cross-references:** See F05 (Build vs. Buy Framework) for TCO/ROIC analysis; see F08 (SaaS Is Dead Narrative) for VC-camp positions McKinsey implicitly counters.

## Executive Summary

McKinsey's published body of work in 2025–2026 presents a nuanced but urgent position: the SaaS model is not dying, but it is undergoing its most fundamental stress test since its own emergence displaced on-premise software. The firm's research across its Technology, Media & Telecommunications practice, QuantumBlack, and the McKinsey Global Institute converges on a consistent thesis — AI shifts competitive advantage away from feature breadth toward proprietary data, workflow integration, and outcome delivery. McKinsey stops short of the VC-circulated narrative that AI enables every enterprise to build bespoke software at near-zero cost, instead framing the question as a "sources of distinctiveness" problem: enterprises should build where differentiation is genuine and buy where speed and proven capability win. McKinsey's February 2026 Frontier Alliance with OpenAI — announced by Global Managing Partner Bob Sternfels — operationalizes this advisory posture by positioning McKinsey as a deployment partner for enterprises using agents alongside existing stacks. The practical implication is a bifurcated SaaS market: incumbents who embed AI deeply will survive and expand; those who do not will face margin compression, switching-cost erosion, and displacement by AI-native entrants.

## Section 4: Build-vs-Buy Advisory Position

McKinsey does not publish a blanket "build" or "buy" recommendation. Its published advisory framework conditions the decision on sources of competitive differentiation.

[FACT]
"Successful scalers will balance build-versus-buy decisions based on sources of distinctiveness and competitive advantage, avoiding technology or vendor lock-in so they can adapt quickly to a fast-evolving offering landscape."
— McKinsey & Company, "Seizing the Agentic AI Advantage"
URL: https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/seizing-the-agentic-ai-advantage
Date: 2025

[FACT]
According to McKinsey's Make-or-Buy Matrix, the primary decision criterion is: "Does this capability offer a unique competitive advantage? If the answer is yes, building internally may make sense. But when speed, cost control, or proven reliability matter more, buying or partnering could be the better strategy."
— McKinsey & Company
URL: https://www.graphapp.ai/blog/build-vs-buy-framework-a-mckinsey-analysis
Date: 2025

[FACT]
"In the emerging agentic age, business-side employees will be able to independently create software assets and manage data through agentic AI, which automates the software development life cycle (SDLC) with oversight from deep specialists."
— McKinsey & Company, "Seizing the Agentic AI Advantage"
URL: https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/seizing-the-agentic-ai-advantage
Date: 2025

[FACT]
"Independent software vendors, tapping their software-as-a-service (SaaS) workflow and business function expertise, and hyperscalers, leveraging their scale and infrastructure stack, are offering managed agentic solutions, bespoke agent deployment services, and agentic marketplaces."
— McKinsey & Company, "Reimagining the Value Proposition of Tech Services for Agentic AI"

## Key Takeaways

1. McKinsey characterizes the AI shift as more disruptive to SaaS than any prior technology wave — but frames the disruption as a business model transformation, not a death sentence for the category. The firm's language is "evolve or be displaced" aimed at SaaS vendors, not "build your own" aimed at enterprise buyers.

2. The firm's central advisory recommendation to enterprise clients is differentiation-conditioned: build only where there is genuine competitive distinctiveness; buy or partner everywhere else. The February 2026 OpenAI Frontier Alliance — signed by Global Managing Partner Bob Sternfels — makes this posture operational: McKinsey's commercial interest is in deploying agents alongside existing stacks, not replacing them.

3. Only 16 percent of SaaS incumbents have monetized AI products. McKinsey treats this gap as both a risk indicator for laggard vendors and a market opportunity for AI-native challengers — not as evidence that enterprises are self-building replacements.

4. McKinsey projects a $200 billion new spending pool in agentic AI services (orchestration, security, governance) — implying sustained vendor markets, not enterprise in-sourcing. The 20–30 percent tech-services contraction it projects targets IT outsourcers, not SaaS platform vendors.

5. Developer productivity gains from AI are large (25–110 percent depending on adoption depth), but McKinsey frames these gains as benefiting both enterprises and software vendors — not as a reason to defect from SaaS. The adoption-to-value gap (88% use AI; only 39% report EBIT impact; 6% are high performers) is McKinsey's central empirical finding — and its strongest implicit argument against premature SaaS replacement narratives.
