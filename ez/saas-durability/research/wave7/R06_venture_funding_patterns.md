# R06: Venture Funding Patterns — What Capital Allocation Reveals About Enterprise Software's Future

**Research Wave:** Wave 7
**Date:** March 2026
**Scope:** VC funding patterns in SaaS vs. AI-native enterprise software, 2024–2026
**Exclusions:** Specific VC firm positions (see Wave 2); public market analysis (see R01–R04)

---

## Executive Summary

Venture capital allocation in 2024–2025 marks a structural reordering of enterprise software investment priorities. AI captured approximately 50% of all global VC dollars in 2025 — totaling $202–211 billion — while traditional SaaS as a standalone funding category contracted at the early stage and saw dramatically lower seed-to-Series A conversion rates. The "great rotation," documented by AngelList data, is not from software to hardware or services, but from human-powered software tooling to AI-native B2B applications. However, absolute SaaS funding on platforms like Carta still grew 25% year-over-year in the first three quarters of 2025, complicating a simple displacement narrative. Critically, historical precedent from the 2021–2022 correction — when 90% of unicorns minted during peak SaaS funding are now valued lower — counsels caution about reading current funding momentum as a reliable prediction of sustained market share outcomes.

## Key Takeaways

- **AI has structurally displaced traditional SaaS at the seed stage.** AI captured 42% of global seed funding in 2025 (up from 30% in 2024), and AngelList data documents a "great rotation" away from non-AI-native software at early-stage deals — though this is a shift within software investment, not away from it.

- **Total enterprise software VC dollars continue to grow, but deal count is falling.** Software VC on platforms like Carta rose 25% YoY in dollar terms through Q3 2025, while the total deal count dropped 18% in 2024 vs. 2023 — indicating capital concentration rather than category abandonment.

- **The Series A crunch disproportionately affects non-AI SaaS founders.** Seed-to-Series A conversion rates fell from 30.6% (2018) to 15.4% (2022 cohort), and seed-stage company closures spiked 102% YoY in Q1 2024, with AI-native startups commanding median deal sizes over $1 million above the market average.

- **Historical funding cycles have been poor predictors of actual market outcomes.** 90% of 2021's unicorns are now valued lower in secondary trades (Forge Global); the dot-com era saw 48% of companies survive despite peak-cycle losses of 27–38%; and machine learning models achieve only 80–89% accuracy in predicting even binary startup outcomes.

- **Geographic concentration in AI is extreme.** The San Francisco Bay Area alone captured $122 billion in AI funding in 2025 — 76% of all U.S. AI investment — while Europe's global VC share contracted from 19% to 13% and Asia remains subdued outside India. Emerging markets (MENA, India) are growing but remain small relative to U.S. AI concentration.
