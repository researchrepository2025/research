# R03: Enterprise IT Budget Trends — SaaS, Custom Development, and AI Investment Shifts

**Research Wave:** Wave 7
**Cross-reference:** See F01 Enterprise SaaS Market Landscape for detailed coverage of SaaS market size and vendor dynamics.
**Scope:** Total IT budget allocation patterns, 2024–2026. Excludes individual company financials (see R04) and TCO analysis (see Wave 9).

---

## Executive Summary

Global enterprise IT spending is on a steep upward trajectory, projected to reach $6.15 trillion in 2026, a 10.8% year-over-year increase driven predominantly by AI infrastructure investment. Enterprise software spending — the category that encompasses SaaS — will grow 14.7% in 2026 to $1.4 trillion, while SaaS specifically is forecast at approximately $300 billion in 2025. The AI investment surge is not uniformly additive: ISG research finds that enterprise AI spending is rising 5.7% while total IT budgets grow just 1.8%, making AI responsible for 30% of all new budget dollars in 2025. CIO survey data from Gartner, Futurum, and Morgan Stanley reveals a reallocation dynamic — CIOs are funding AI primarily by consolidating vendors, eliminating low-ROI SaaS tools, and reducing headcount growth rather than securing net-new budget. Whether AI spending is cannibalistic or additive to SaaS budgets remains contested: Gartner's software segment (which includes SaaS) is still growing in absolute dollars, but CIOs are paying higher prices for fewer, more capable platforms rather than expanding their software portfolios.

## Key Takeaways

- **IT spending is accelerating but unevenly distributed.** Gartner forecasts worldwide IT spending reaching $6.15 trillion in 2026 (+10.8%), but data center infrastructure and AI absorb most of the growth — not enterprise software or SaaS.

- **SaaS spending is growing in absolute dollars but faces structural pressure.** SaaS reached approximately $300 billion in 2025 and may approach $315 billion in 2026, but this growth is partially driven by AI-attached price increases of 20–37% at renewal rather than net-new seat expansion. The enterprise portfolio count has fallen 18% since 2022.

- **AI budget sourcing is a hybrid of additive and reallocation.** Morgan Stanley finds a majority of CIOs describe AI as funded by net-new dollars; ISG finds only 1.8% total IT budget growth against 5.7% AI spending growth — making AI mathematically responsible for crowding out lower-priority spend. Both dynamics operate simultaneously.

- **Headcount is the primary sacrifice on the altar of AI investment.** Gartner CFO data shows headcount growth collapsing from 6% in 2025 to 2% in 2026, while 75% of CFOs plan to increase technology budgets — a clear substitution effect occurring at the organizational rather than the SaaS-line-item level.

- **Incumbent SaaS vendors are capturing AI budget, not losing it.** Gartner's "Trough of Disillusionment" framing for 2026 predicts AI will be sold to enterprises by incumbent software providers. Combined with 68% of CIOs consolidating to fewer vendors (not abandoning SaaS), the picture is platform concentration within SaaS, not SaaS displacement.
