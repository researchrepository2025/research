## C01 — McKinsey Perspectives

**Rating: PASS**

**Scope adherence:** The file stays cleanly within McKinsey-published material. Two pre-2025 sources (June 2024 Gnanasambandam paper, June 2023 MGI productivity report) are explicitly tagged `[PRE-2025]` and justified as foundational frameworks cited in later 2025 publications. The McKinsey-via-third-party citation in Section 4 (`graphapp.ai` summarizing the Make-or-Buy Matrix) is the one instance where the sourcing is one step removed from a primary McKinsey URL — this is a minor issue but not a disqualifying one given the attribution is clearly flagged.

**Expert attribution:** Strong. Named partners include Jeremy Schneider (Senior Partner, Global Co-Head Software Practice), Alex Singla (Senior Partner, Chicago, Global Leader QuantumBlack), Alexander Sukharevsky (Senior Partner, London), Lareina Yee (Senior Partner, Bay Area), Mohit Khanna, Nimish Mittal, Joshan Abraham, Naveen Sastry, Bob Sternfels (Global Managing Partner), Virginia Simmons (Global Leader Alliances), and Chandra Gnanasambandam (with a note that he departed to SailPoint in February 2025). Titles and practice affiliations are provided for all named individuals.

**Inline citations:** Every claim — including statistics, quotes, and interpretive facts — carries an inline URL and date. No claim is left to the sources section alone. The [FACT], [STATISTIC], [QUOTE], and [DATA POINT] tagging system creates a clear audit trail.

**Balance:** The file presents McKinsey's nuanced "transformation not extinction" position accurately. It documents both the optimistic signals (AI high performers achieving 110%+ productivity gains) and the sobering ones (only 39% of organizations report EBIT impact at scale; enterprises retrenching from failed agentic builds). The advisory bias risk — McKinsey's own February 2026 OpenAI Frontier Alliance — is noted in the executive summary but not developed as a conflict-of-interest caveat with the same rigor that C04 applies to the Big 4. This is a minor gap for synthesis.

**Substantiveness:** Key findings are specific and quantified: the 16% SaaS commercialization rate, 80%+ LLM cost decline per year, 5–10 percentage point vendor switching acceleration, $200B tech services contraction risk, 20–30% tech services contraction tied to 200 C-suite executives surveyed in July 2025. No vague platitudes in the key findings section.

**Notes for synthesis:** The graphapp.ai Make-or-Buy Matrix citation should be flagged as secondary source at synthesis. The McKinsey-OpenAI commercial relationship should be noted explicitly as a potential bias vector when comparing McKinsey's "deploy agents alongside existing stacks" advisory posture to pure-play build positions.

All seven source articles are from `bain.com` or Bain press releases, covering Technology Report 2025 (multiple chapters), the "Great Debate" article, the SaaS stocks brief, and the ERP/agentic AI transformation paper. No scope creep to non-Bain sources.

**Expert attribution:** Exceptional. The file includes a dedicated "Named Bain Partners" table with 20 named partners, their titles ("Partner"), office locations, and specific article attributions. In-body attribution is also consistent: Chuck Whitten (Partner, Dallas), David Crawford (Chairman, Global Technology Practice, Silicon Valley), Anne Hoecker (Head, Global Technology Practice, Silicon Valley), Pascal Gautheron (Partner, Paris), Florian Braun (Partner, London), and others all carry full identification on first use. This is the strongest expert attribution of the four files.

**Inline citations:** Every [FACT], [STATISTIC], and [QUOTE] block contains a URL and date. The survey methodology is documented for the key ERP survey (n=480, Bain Technology Maturity Assessment Benchmarking Survey, 2025). No orphaned claims.

**Balance:** Bain's five-scenario framework for SaaS disruption — ranging from "No AI" to "AI cannibalizes SaaS" — is documented without editorializing which scenario is correct. The file notes that Bain does not treat the full-cannibalization scenario as the modal outcome, which is an accurate reflection of Bain's published position. Section 3's market data (gross retention still ~90%, net revenue retention stalling) presents both supportive and cautionary signals. No advocacy for a particular outcome is present.

The build-vs-buy framework is rendered as a specific seven-factor decision rubric with delivery model guidance per use case type.

**Notes for synthesis:** The "$2 trillion needed to fund AI scaling" statistic from Section 1 is infrastructure-economics context rather than SaaS-durability evidence — it may require framing at synthesis to avoid it being read as direct commentary on SaaS market health. The Claude Cowork SaaS stock drop attribution (Section 3) should be verified as the causal event Bain specifies, since causation to a single product launch is a strong claim.

The scope header correctly notes this. All other material is 2025–2026. The file also correctly pulls BCG Henderson Institute, BCG/MIT Sloan joint publications, and BCGonTech Medium articles — all within the BCG publishing ecosystem and appropriate to the scope.

**Expert attribution:** Strong. Named individuals include Nicolas de Bellefonds (Managing Director & Senior Partner, Global Leader of BCG AI, Paris), Akash Bhatia (Managing Director & Senior Partner, Global Sector Leader Technology, Silicon Valley), Sesh Iyer (Managing Director & Senior Partner, North America Chair BCG X, Global Leader BCG Henderson Institute AI & Technology Lab), Shervin Khodabandeh (Managing Director & Senior Partner, leader of BCG's AI business), Amanda Luther (Managing Director & Senior Partner, Austin), Rich Lesser (Global Chair, BCG), Sam Ransbotham (Analytics Professor, Boston College, BCG Henderson Institute co-author). The Section 5 partner camps framework usefully clusters individuals by analytical position rather than just listing them.

**Inline citations:** Every claim carries an inline URL and date. The comparative data table in Section 13 is well-constructed, mapping every metric to a source and date. Large multi-author studies (Widening AI Value Gap: 15 named authors; BCG/MIT Sloan: 5 named authors) are attributed in full on first use. Survey methodology is documented for all major data points (n=1,250+, n=2,102, n=602).

**Balance:** The file explicitly distinguishes BCG's actual thesis (pricing model disruption, not SaaS category collapse) from more extreme positions, which is accurate to the source material. The pre-2025 Kropp quote on consumption-based pricing is characterized as BCG's earliest public statement of a thesis, not as current-year guidance, which is appropriately cautious. The "Advisory Firm Bias" section cross-references (via C04) BCG's OpenAI partnership as a commercial factor, and the file's executive summary acknowledges BCG's own AI services revenue ($2.7B) as context. The file neither overstates the SaaS durability case nor alarmism.

**Substantiveness:** The file is the most data-dense of the four. The Section 13 comparative table alone contains 30 quantified metrics. The five-tier pricing model taxonomy in Section 4 is concrete and sourced.

**Notes for synthesis:** The file's breadth (13 sections) is a strength for comprehensive coverage but may require selective citation at synthesis to avoid drowning the narrative in BCG data. The one attribution gap is the comparative data table row "AI-native firm revenue per employee vs. traditional: 25–35x more" — the inline source points to a "BCG CEO roadmap" article, but this specific claim should be verified against the cited URL, as it is an extreme figure that could distort synthesis if misread.

## C04 — Big 4 Advisory Firms (Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, EY)

**Rating: MINOR_ISSUES**

**Scope adherence:** The file covers Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, and EY within the 2025–2026 boundary. Coverage is uneven across the four firms. Deloitte and EY receive substantive section treatment with multiple sourced claims each. PwC's coverage is thin — five statistics and facts, two of which (cloud threats, cyber risk) are only tangentially related to SaaS durability. Accenture is covered adequately for its survey statistics but its strategic position on the build-vs-buy question is not extracted, even though the Technology Vision 2025 reportedly contains relevant material. The scope boundary itself (four firms in one file) creates an inherent depth tradeoff, but PwC's coverage is notably thinner than the other three and may not give an accurate picture of PwC's published position on SaaS specifically.

**Expert attribution:** This is the file's primary weakness. EY is the sole firm with a named individual — Michael Stricklen (Managing Director, Software Strategy Group, EY-Parthenon) — and all EY quotes are attributed to him by name. Deloitte, Accenture, and PwC sections contain no named authors on any quote, statistic, or claim; all attribution stops at the firm and publication level. For Deloitte, this is partly explained by the Tech Trends and Software Industry Outlook reports being published without named individual authors. However, Accenture's Technology Vision 2025 press release does name specific Accenture leaders, and their absence from the file is a gap. The executive summary's comparison ("EY is the only firm among the four to publish named-author analysis") is accurate but highlights a deficiency the file should flag more directly as a sourcing limitation rather than presenting it as a differentiator.

**Inline citations:** All claims in the Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, and EY sections carry inline URLs and dates. The "Common Themes Across All Four Firms" section uses a single combined URL string covering all four sources simultaneously, which is structurally clunky but traceable. The Advisory Firm Bias section is well-sourced, with specific URLs for EY's SAP S/4HANA Cloud launch, PwC's C3.ai alliance, and Deloitte's margin-framing observation.

**Balance:** The file's Advisory Firm Bias section is a genuine contribution — it documents commercial conflicts (EY's SAP implementation revenue, PwC's C3.ai alliance, Accenture's potential sample bias in its own survey) that the other C-file series files do not include. This balances the otherwise forward-looking advisory framing. One tension: the executive summary states "a consistent structural thesis has emerged: agentic AI will erode — and in some scenarios replace — traditional SaaS as the dominant enterprise software delivery model" but the Key Takeaways section immediately qualifies this by noting none of the four firms recommends reducing SaaS investment. The apparent contradiction should be resolved or clarified at synthesis.

**Substantiveness:** Deloitte and Accenture findings are specific and quantified (60% of TAM by 2030, 77% of executives, 40% AI agent integration by end of 2026, $35B agentic AI market by 2030). PwC's section lacks comparable specificity on SaaS durability — its two on-topic claims (SaaS portfolio inflation, multi-cloud architecture priority) are useful but the section reads more like CIO priorities context than a PwC position on SaaS specifically. EY's quotes are specific about transformation requirements but lean toward advisory language ("must undergo significant transformation") rather than substantive predictions or data.

**Notes for synthesis:** The PwC section should be flagged at synthesis as incomplete relative to PwC's published record on this topic — PwC publishes annual AI Business Survey data that may contain more direct SaaS durability commentary than was captured here. The Accenture section should note whether the Technology Vision 2025 full PDF was reviewed for build-vs-buy content beyond the survey statistics, since the file only references the web summary and press release. The executive summary's "erode — and in some scenarios replace" framing overstates the modal Big 4 position relative to what the file actually documents.

## Cross-File Observations for Synthesis Team

1. **Convergent thesis:** All four files document a "transformation not extinction" consensus across McKinsey, Bain, BCG, Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, and EY. No major consulting firm publishes a "SaaS is dead" position. This consensus should be presented in synthesis as a data point about professional advisory incentive alignment, not as authoritative proof of SaaS durability.

2. **Commercial conflict pattern:** McKinsey (OpenAI Frontier Alliance), BCG (OpenAI partnership, $2.7B AI services revenue), Deloitte, Accenture, PwC (C3.ai alliance), and EY (SAP S/4HANA implementation revenue) all have direct financial exposure to enterprise software implementation engagements. C04 documents this most explicitly; the conflict is implicit in C01–C03. Synthesis should treat consulting firm positions as a coherent camp with shared commercial incentives, not as independent corroboration.

3. **Build-vs-buy frameworks:** Bain (seven-factor decision matrix), BCG (four-option portfolio), and McKinsey (sources-of-distinctiveness matrix) each publish formal decision frameworks. These are suitable for direct comparison in the synthesis F05 (Build vs. Buy Framework) file. C04 does not extract equivalent frameworks from Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, or EY — this is a gap if the synthesis requires complete coverage.

4. **Data quality variation:** BCG and Bain cite primary survey data with sample sizes (n=480, n=602, n=1,250+, n=2,102). McKinsey and Deloitte cite proprietary research without always specifying sample sizes. Accenture's n=4,000+ is documented. PwC's May 2025 Pulse survey is referenced without a sample size in C04. Statistical weight should be adjusted accordingly in synthesis.
