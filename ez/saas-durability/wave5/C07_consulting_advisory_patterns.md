# C07: Consulting Advisory Patterns — SaaS vs. Build
## Enterprise SaaS Durability Project | Wave 5 Research

**Research Question:** What are consulting firms actually advising enterprise clients to do about SaaS vs. build — beyond their public positions?

**Scope:** Advisory patterns and incentives across the consulting industry. Not specific firm positions.

**Sources:** 2025–2026 only. All claims inline-cited.

---

## Executive Summary

The consulting industry is publicly neutral on SaaS vs. build, but the structural economics tell a different story. Implementation engagements — whether configuring purchased SaaS or building custom software — generate 3–5x more billable revenue than pure strategy advisory. As agentic coding tools compress software development timelines from months to days, consulting firms face a fundamental business model disruption while simultaneously advising clients on decisions that directly affect consulting revenue. Three structural tensions define the current moment: (1) consulting firms earn more from complexity, while both mature SaaS and agentic builds reduce complexity; (2) the "center of excellence" advisory pattern creates durable, multi-year revenue streams regardless of the build-vs-buy outcome; and (3) firms are now deploying the very agentic tools that threaten their billable-hour model in client delivery, creating a revenue arbitrage they have not yet publicly disclosed.

## 4. Revenue Model Implications: Do Consulting Firms Benefit More from Build or Buy?

[QUOTE]
"The problem arises when consulting firms receive revenue from both buyers and sellers — it is difficult to maintain integrity in doing so. If enough money is involved, it can be tempting for consultants to think 'It would be good for us if this vendor could win this deal,' because it is an easier way to make money."
— Frank S. Scavo, Conflicts of Interest at Three Levels, Enterprise Spectator
URL: https://enterprisespectator.substack.com/p/conflicts-of-interest-at-three-levels
Date: January 31, 2025

[QUOTE]
"One best practice for buyers in choosing a consulting firm is to ask, upfront, what percentage of your total firm revenue comes from tech providers vs. tech buyers — though you may not get a truthful answer."
— Frank S. Scavo, Conflicts of Interest at Three Levels, Enterprise Spectator
URL: https://enterprisespectator.substack.com/p/conflicts-of-interest-at-three-levels
Date: January 31, 2025

[FACT]
The Enterprise Spectator article identifies three tiers of consulting conflicts of interest: (1) Illegal — bribery, breach of fiduciary duty; (2) Legal but unethical — undisclosed commissions, steering to vendors offering subcontracting opportunities; (3) Legal and ethical but indirect — revenue from tech providers; dedicated implementation teams for specific vendors.
— Frank S. Scavo, Enterprise Spectator
URL: https://enterprisespectator.substack.com/p/conflicts-of-interest-at-three-levels
Date: January 31, 2025

[FACT]
Detection red flags for conflicted consulting advice identified in primary source include: "Excessive praise or criticism of specific vendors," "Partner/alliance pages listing tech providers," "Vendor-specific implementation service offerings."
— Frank S. Scavo, Enterprise Spectator
URL: https://enterprisespectator.substack.com/p/conflicts-of-interest-at-three-levels
Date: January 31, 2025

[QUOTE]
"If your technology advisor has commercial relationships with tech vendors, they are going to be biased toward those vendors."
